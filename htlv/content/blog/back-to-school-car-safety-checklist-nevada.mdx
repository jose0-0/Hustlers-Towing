The start of the school year in Nevada brings a specific kind of traffic pressure. More cars on the road at the same times every day, school zones with reduced speed limits and crossing guards, parents in a hurry, and kids who are late. It also coincides with the tail end of Las Vegas summer — a season that is genuinely brutal on vehicles.

Before the routine of school drop-offs and pickups gets going in full, it is worth spending an hour running through the basics on the family vehicle. A breakdown during the school run is one of the more stressful possible outcomes, and most of them are preventable.

## Why Back-to-School Timing Matters for Car Maintenance

Nevada schools in the Las Vegas valley typically start in early August, which means the first weeks of the school year happen during peak summer heat. Vehicles that have been sitting in driveways, garages, and parking lots all summer have been under thermal stress for months.

By the time September arrives, the battery is at its most degraded point of the year. Tire pressure has been fluctuating with daily temperature swings. Fluids have been working hard. Wiper blades that have been baking in the sun since spring may be cracked and useless.

The school run adds daily mileage, often in stop-and-go conditions during rush hour, which is harder on vehicles than highway driving. Short trips that do not fully warm up the engine also prevent the alternator from fully recharging the battery, which compounds any battery weakness that developed over summer.

## The Checklist

**Battery.** If your battery is more than two years old and has not been tested recently, get it tested before the school year gets into full swing. It is free at any auto parts store and takes five minutes. A battery that is marginal going into fall is likely to fail sometime during the school year when you least need it.

**Tires.** Check the pressure on all four tires and the spare. With September temperatures still in the 90s and 100s in Las Vegas, tire pressure needs to be checked in the morning before driving for an accurate reading. Also do a quick visual inspection of the tread and sidewalls — look for anything embedded in the tire, any visible cracking, or uneven wear that might suggest an alignment issue.

**Oil.** If an oil change is coming up, do it before the school routine starts rather than during it. A fresh oil change going into the higher daily mileage of the school run is better than putting it off.

**Coolant level.** Check the overflow reservoir with the engine cold. If it is low, top it off. If it keeps needing to be topped off, there is a leak that should be looked at before cooler weather obscures the problem.

**Brakes.** If your brakes have been making noise — squealing, grinding, or a pulsing feeling when you slow down — have them inspected before the school year. School zones require frequent stops and predictable braking behavior.

**Wiper blades.** Las Vegas monsoon season runs through September. If your wiper blades are leaving streaks or skipping across the windshield, replace them before the first storm catches you on the school run.

**Lights.** Walk around the car and check that all lights are working — headlights, taillights, brake lights, and turn signals. School zone and crossing guard visibility depends on other drivers being able to see your signals clearly.

**Emergency kit.** Make sure your roadside kit is stocked. Water, a flashlight with working batteries, basic tools, a phone charger, and jumper cables or a portable jump starter are the basics. With kids in the car during the school run, having these items is more important than it might be for a solo driver.

## School Zone Driving Reminders

This is not strictly car maintenance but it is worth saying. School zones in Nevada have reduced speed limits that are strictly enforced during arrival and dismissal times. The fines for school zone violations in Nevada are significant.

Distracted driving near schools — checking a phone, eating, or anything that takes attention off the road — is especially dangerous when pedestrian traffic from students and parents is at its highest.

If you are dropping off or picking up inside a school parking lot or loop, follow the school's specific traffic pattern. They are designed to manage the volume and keep kids safe, and cutting through the wrong way or stopping in the wrong place creates backups and risks.

## If You Break Down During the School Run

If your car breaks down during drop-off or pickup, the first priority is getting safely off the road and out of the traffic flow around the school. School zones have a lot of pedestrian activity and disabled vehicles create real hazards.

If your kids are in the car, keep them inside with you until help arrives or until you have moved to a safe location well clear of traffic.
