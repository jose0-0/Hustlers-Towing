When something goes wrong with your car, the first question most people ask is who to call. The answer depends on what is actually happening, and the difference between needing a tow and needing roadside assistance is more significant than most drivers realize — both in terms of what happens to your car and what it costs.

Here is a clear breakdown of the two, what each covers, and how to quickly figure out which situation you are in.

## What Towing Is

Towing means your vehicle is loaded onto or hooked up to a truck and transported to a different location. The car is not driven — it rides. The destination is typically a repair shop, a dealership, your home, or a storage facility.

You need a tow when the vehicle cannot or should not be driven. This includes a major mechanical failure where driving would cause further damage, a serious accident where the vehicle is not driveable, a flat tire in a situation where a roadside tire change is not safe, a dead battery that cannot be jumped, or any situation where the car is immobile.

## What Roadside Assistance Is

Roadside assistance means someone comes to your location and resolves the problem on the spot so that you can drive away. The car stays with you. Common roadside assistance services include jump starts, fuel delivery, lockout service, flat tire changes using your spare, and minor roadside repairs.

The goal of roadside assistance is to fix the immediate problem in the field so you do not need a tow at all. When it works, it is faster and cheaper than a tow because your car ends up at your destination rather than at a shop.

## The Quick Way to Tell Which You Need

Ask yourself one question: if the immediate problem were resolved, could I drive this car safely?

If the answer is yes, you probably need roadside assistance. Dead battery — a jump start gets you driving. Locked out — a lockout service gets you in the car and driving. Out of gas — a fuel delivery gets you moving. Flat tire with a functional spare — a tire change gets you to a tire shop.

If the answer is no, you need a tow. Engine that has seized or overheated severely. Transmission failure. Accident damage that affects steering, brakes, or structural integrity. A flat tire with no spare, or a blowout that damaged the wheel. Any situation where driving the car would make the underlying problem worse.

## The Gray Areas

Some situations are not immediately obvious.

A slow battery is a good example. If you get a jump start and the car starts, you can drive to an auto parts store and get the battery replaced on the spot. Jump start, no tow needed. But if you get a jump and the car dies again within a few minutes of driving, the alternator may be failing and driving it further could leave you stranded again — at that point a tow to a mechanic is the smarter move.

A minor fender bender is another gray area. If the car is driveable and nothing is dragging or leaking, you can typically drive it. If there is fluid on the ground under the car or the front end is close enough to a tire that it might contact during a turn, tow it.

Engine warning lights are a common source of confusion. A check engine light alone, with no other symptoms, typically does not require a tow. The car can be driven to a shop for diagnosis. But a check engine light combined with unusual noises, loss of power, or other symptoms means pull over and assess before driving further.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does roadside assistance cost less than a tow?**

Generally yes. A jump start, lockout service, or fuel delivery typically runs $50 to $100 in Las Vegas. A tow adds mileage charges on top of the base fee. If roadside assistance can solve your problem, it is almost always the cheaper option.

**Can I request roadside assistance and then decide I need a tow instead?**

Yes. If the roadside technician arrives and determines the problem cannot be resolved on the spot, they can arrange a tow for you. You would pay for the roadside call and then separately for the tow if the vehicle needs to be transported.
