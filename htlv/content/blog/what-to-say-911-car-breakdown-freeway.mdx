Most freeway breakdowns in Nevada do not require a 911 call. If your car dies on the shoulder, your hazards are on, and you are safely out of traffic, calling a towing company directly is the right move. 911 is for emergencies, and tying up those lines for a non-emergency breakdown means someone else with an actual emergency waits longer.

That said, there are situations on Nevada freeways where calling 911 first is exactly the right call. Knowing the difference, and knowing what to say when you do call, can make a real difference in how quickly and safely the situation gets resolved.

## When to Call 911 First

Call 911 before calling a towing company when any of the following are true.

Someone is injured. This includes anyone in your vehicle and anyone else involved if you were in an accident. Medical response needs to be coordinated and 911 handles that.

Your car is stopped in a live lane of traffic and you cannot get it to the shoulder. This is a genuinely dangerous situation that requires law enforcement to manage traffic and get you protected. Do not wait — call immediately.

There is fire or smoke coming from the vehicle. Get away from the car first, then call 911. Vehicle fires can escalate very quickly.

You were involved in an accident with another vehicle. Nevada law requires that accidents involving injury or significant property damage be reported to law enforcement.

You feel unsafe in your location — an isolated area at night, someone approaching your car who feels threatening, or any situation where you feel personally at risk.

For everything else — car died on the shoulder, tire blew and you got safely pulled over, battery died in a parking lot — call a towing company directly. It is faster and it keeps 911 lines clear.

## What the Dispatcher Needs From You

If you do need to call 911, the faster you can give the dispatcher accurate information, the faster help arrives. Here is what they will need and in what order it matters.

Your location comes first and is the most important piece of information. On a freeway, the most useful location information is the highway number, your direction of travel, and the nearest mile marker. Mile markers are the small green signs posted along Nevada freeways at one-mile intervals. They will have a number like "MM 42" or simply a number on the post. That number tells the dispatcher exactly where you are on the highway.

If you cannot find a mile marker, give the nearest exit name or number that you passed. Saying "I just passed the Flamingo Road exit on southbound I-15" is enough for dispatch to narrow down your location quickly.

If you have no landmarks, open Google Maps on your phone. Your blue dot location pin can give you coordinates, or you can read out the name of the closest road intersection the app is showing.

After location, tell them what happened. Car accident, medical emergency, vehicle fire, car stopped in a live lane — be specific and brief. They will ask follow-up questions.

Tell them how many people are involved and whether anyone is injured. This determines what response they send.

Stay on the line unless the dispatcher tells you to end the call. They may need to ask follow-up questions, and staying connected allows them to update responders as the situation develops.

## What to Do While You Wait for Response

If you are in the car and it is safe to stay there, stay buckled in and wait. A running vehicle with its hazards on is more visible than a person standing on the shoulder.

If you are outside the vehicle because it is unsafe to remain inside — smoke, fire, or a car that is in a position where it could be hit — get as far off the road as possible. Get behind a guardrail if one is available. The space between the guardrail and the road is safer than the shoulder in most cases.

Keep your phone available and do not let the battery die if you can help it.

## After Law Enforcement Arrives

When Nevada Highway Patrol or Las Vegas Metropolitan Police arrive, they will assess the situation, assist with immediate safety, and in many cases will help arrange or authorize a tow. In some situations, law enforcement will call a tow company from their contracted list.

If you have a preference for which towing company handles your vehicle, you can request it. Law enforcement cannot force you to use a specific company if you have an alternative you prefer, though if your car is blocking traffic they may need to act quickly.

Once the immediate situation is safe and you are with law enforcement, you can coordinate the tow to your preferred destination.
