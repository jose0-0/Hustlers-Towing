Handing your car over to a stranger when you are stressed, stranded, and possibly in a dangerous location requires a baseline of trust that is not always easy to establish quickly. Most tow truck drivers are professionals doing an honest job. But not all of them are, and knowing what to look for gives you a way to make a fast, informed assessment when the truck pulls up.

Here are six things worth checking, most of which you can evaluate in the first two minutes after the driver arrives.

## 1. The Truck Is Marked With the Company Name

A professional towing company puts their name and contact information on their trucks. This is standard practice across the industry and it is how you verify that the truck that showed up is actually from the company you called.

If an unmarked vehicle shows up claiming to be from the company you called, ask the driver to confirm the company name and give you the dispatch phone number to verify. A professional driver will not be offended by this request. If they are evasive or resistant, that is information worth paying attention to.

Unmarked trucks are more common in situations where dispatch brokers farm out jobs to independent contractors. The contractor may run a legitimate operation but has no visible connection to the company you called, which makes verification harder.

## 2. The Driver Can Confirm Your Call Details

When the driver arrives, they should be able to confirm the basic details of your service call — your name, the vehicle description, the pickup location, and the destination. This information was relayed to them from the dispatcher when the job was assigned.

A driver who arrives and does not seem to know the details of your specific call, or who asks you to confirm information in a way that suggests they are hearing it for the first time, may have been dispatched through a broker system where communication was incomplete.

## 3. They Do a Walkaround Before Touching the Vehicle

A professional driver walks around your vehicle before loading it, notes any pre-existing damage, and gives you the opportunity to do the same. This protects you both — it establishes what was there before the tow so there is no dispute after.

A driver who immediately starts hooking up without any discussion of the vehicle's condition is skipping a step that professional operators do consistently. It is not automatically a sign of bad intent, but it is worth asking them to do a quick walkaround before they start if they have not done it themselves.

## 4. They Confirm the Destination Before Loading

Before your car gets hooked up or loaded, the driver should confirm where the vehicle is going. This is your last easy opportunity to make sure everyone is on the same page about the destination.

A driver who starts loading before confirming the destination is getting ahead of a basic step. Once your car is on the truck and moving, redirecting it to a different destination creates complications. Confirm the destination out loud — street address or business name — before loading begins.

## 5. The Rate Matches What You Were Quoted

If you got a rate quote from the dispatcher before the truck was sent, the driver's bill should match it. The hook-up fee, the per-mile rate, and any surcharges should align with what you were told on the call.

If the driver presents a bill that is significantly different from what was quoted — with new fees that were never mentioned — ask for an explanation before you pay. Get the itemized invoice and compare it line by line to what you were told. If something does not add up and the driver cannot explain it satisfactorily, you have the right to call the company directly before paying.

## 6. They Answer Your Questions Without Irritation

A professional driver handles the interaction with you professionally. That means being patient with questions about the process, being willing to explain what they are doing and why, and not treating you like an inconvenience for wanting to understand what is happening with your car.

Being stressed and asking questions is a normal response to being broken down on the side of the road. A driver who is short-tempered, dismissive of your concerns, or who pressures you to hurry up and sign without reviewing the paperwork is showing you something about how they operate.

You do not need the interaction to be warm or particularly friendly. You need it to be professional, clear, and honest. Those three things together tell you a lot about the driver and the company they represent.
