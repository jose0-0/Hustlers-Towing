Jump-starting a car is one of those things most drivers learn once and then forget the details of until they actually need to do it. In most climates, the standard process works fine. In Las Vegas summer heat, there are a few things worth knowing before you pop the hood, because extreme heat changes what a battery can and can't handle.

Here's a practical guide to jump-starting a car safely when it's 110 degrees outside, including the steps most guides skip.

## Why Heat Makes Jump-Starts Riskier

Lead-acid car batteries don't love extreme heat. High temperatures cause the electrolyte solution inside the battery to evaporate over time, weakening the battery's capacity. A battery that's been sitting in a Las Vegas summer is often already stressed even before it dies.

The risk during a jump-start is that an overheated or severely degraded battery can, in rare cases, emit hydrogen gas. Hydrogen is flammable. A spark near a venting battery is a potential ignition source. This isn't a reason to never jump-start a car, but it is a reason to follow the steps carefully and not take shortcuts.

The other issue is heat on the cables and connection points. In summer, the components under the hood are already hot. Handle everything deliberately and don't rush.

## What You Need

A set of jumper cables in good condition with no cracked insulation or corroded clamps, or a portable jump starter pack. If you're using cables, you need a second vehicle with a working battery. Make sure the second vehicle's battery is a similar or higher voltage than yours — in almost all cases both will be 12V, but it's worth confirming.

## Before You Connect Anything

Check the dead battery visually before you start. If it's cracked, leaking, or has visible corrosion that's built up heavily around the terminals, do not attempt a jump-start. A battery in that condition needs to be replaced, not jumped. Call us instead.

Also check that neither battery terminal is hot to the touch. On a very hot day in Las Vegas this may be unavoidable, but if one terminal is dramatically hotter than expected, that's a sign of a problem inside the battery.

Make sure both vehicles are turned off before connecting anything.

## The Correct Connection Order

This is the part people get wrong most often. The order matters.

Connect the red clamp to the positive terminal of the dead battery first. The positive terminal is usually marked with a plus sign or the letters POS.

Connect the other red clamp to the positive terminal of the good battery.

Connect the black clamp to the negative terminal of the good battery.

Connect the last black clamp to an unpainted metal surface on the engine block or frame of the dead vehicle, away from the battery. This is the ground connection. Connecting it away from the battery reduces the chance of a spark near any hydrogen gas the battery might be venting.

## Starting the Cars

Start the vehicle with the good battery and let it run for two to three minutes. This gives the dead battery time to take on some charge before you try to start it.

Now try to start the dead vehicle. If it starts, great. If it doesn't start after two or three attempts, the battery may be too far gone to jump, or there may be another issue beyond the battery.

## Disconnecting the Cables

Disconnect in the reverse order from how you connected. Remove the black ground clamp from the previously dead vehicle first, then the black clamp from the good battery, then the red clamp from the good battery, then the red clamp from the vehicle you just started.

Keep the jumper cables clear of moving engine parts while you disconnect. In the heat and the rush of getting things working, this is an easy step to get careless about.

## After the Jump-Start

Drive the jumped vehicle for at least 20 to 30 minutes without turning it off. The alternator needs time to recharge the battery. Short trips won't do it. If you start the car and immediately turn it off to run into a store, you'll likely be back in the same situation next time.

After the drive, get the battery tested. Most auto parts stores in Las Vegas test batteries for free. If the battery is below a healthy threshold, replace it before it leaves you stranded again. A battery that needed a jump once in summer is a battery that will need another jump soon.

If you've already tried jumping it and it won't start or starts and immediately dies, which could mean the alternator is the real problem. If you don't have cables and no one nearby does either.
