You called a tow truck, the driver showed up, did the job, and then handed you a bill that seemed higher than you expected. Or maybe you're trying to figure out what a tow is going to cost before you even make the call.

Either way, towing bills aren't random — there's a real structure behind how they're calculated. Once you understand it, the numbers make sense.

Here's exactly how towing costs work in Nevada, what the standard components are, and what red flags to watch for.

## The Base Hook-Up Fee

Every tow starts with a hook-up fee, sometimes called a dispatch fee or drop fee. This is a flat charge just for the truck showing up and connecting to your vehicle — it covers the driver's time and the cost of getting the equipment to your location.

In Las Vegas, hook-up fees typically run between $50 and $100 for a standard passenger vehicle. This number can vary based on the company, the type of vehicle being towed, and the type of truck required. A flatbed dispatch for a luxury or all-wheel-drive vehicle will usually carry a higher base fee than a standard wheel-lift tow of a basic sedan.

The hook-up fee applies regardless of how far you're going. Even if your destination is two blocks away, the base charge is the same.

## The Per-Mile Rate

After the hook-up fee, most towing companies charge a per-mile rate for the actual distance towed. In Nevada, standard per-mile rates for passenger vehicles generally run between $3 and $7 per mile, with flatbed rates at the higher end of that range.

The mileage is typically calculated from the point of pickup to the drop-off location — not from where the truck started. So if you break down on I-15 near the airport and need to get to a shop in Henderson, you're paying for those miles, not for the truck's drive from its garage to you.

For short local tows within Las Vegas, the mileage charge is often modest. Where it adds up is on longer hauls — getting a vehicle from one end of the valley to the other, or out to a specialized shop that may not be near the breakdown point.

## Time of Day and Day of Week

This is one the most common reasons people are surprised by their bill. Towing rates are not flat across all hours. Most companies charge premium rates for:

After-hours service, typically anything outside of standard business hours like evenings and early mornings. Holiday and weekend rates, which can apply on major holidays and sometimes on Sundays. Emergency response surcharges for situations involving accidents, disabled vehicles in active lanes, or calls that require expedited response.

In Las Vegas, where the city runs 24 hours, this matters more than in most markets. A breakdown at 2am on a Saturday night on the Strip is going to cost more than the same breakdown at 2pm on a Tuesday in a residential neighborhood. That's not a scam — it's a real cost difference that every legitimate towing company applies.

## Vehicle Type and Tow Method

Not all vehicles cost the same to tow, and not all situations allow for the same tow method.

Standard passenger cars towed via wheel-lift are the baseline. Flatbed towing costs more because it requires more specialized equipment and takes longer to load and secure properly. Flatbed is typically required for all-wheel-drive vehicles, vehicles with low clearance, luxury and exotic cars, motorcycles, and any vehicle that has sustained undercarriage damage.

Larger vehicles — full-size trucks, SUVs, vans, and commercial vehicles — also carry higher rates because they require heavier equipment and more time.

If your vehicle needs to be winched out of a ditch, pulled from a parking structure with height restrictions, or recovered from off-road terrain, expect additional fees on top of the standard tow rate. These specialized services require extra equipment, extra time, and additional risk for the driver.

## Storage Fees

If your vehicle needs to be stored at the tow yard before you can arrange pickup or repairs, storage fees begin accruing — usually per day. Nevada has regulations governing how towing companies must notify vehicle owners and what storage fees can be charged, but the fees can add up quickly if the vehicle sits for several days.

The best way to avoid storage fees is to know where you want your vehicle to go before you call the tow truck. Have a mechanic in mind, confirm they can accept the vehicle, and tell the driver at the time of pickup. A direct drop at an open shop means no storage fee at all.

## What a Reasonable Bill Looks Like

To make this concrete, here's how a typical Las Vegas tow adds up for a standard passenger vehicle during normal hours.

A local tow of around 10 miles — the kind that covers most intra-valley situations — would typically run somewhere between $100 and $150 total. A longer haul of 25 or 30 miles would land closer to $175 to $250 depending on the company and vehicle type. An after-hours tow with a flatbed for an all-wheel-drive vehicle over a similar distance could easily reach $250 to $350 or more.

These are general ranges, not guarantees. Actual rates vary by company. What matters is that you ask for an estimate before the truck arrives and confirm the rate structure — any reputable company will give you this information upfront.

## How to Avoid Getting Overcharged

The towing industry has a real problem with predatory pricing in some markets. Las Vegas is no exception. Here's how to protect yourself.

Ask for a quote before the truck arrives. A legitimate towing company will give you a hook-up fee and per-mile rate over the phone. If they refuse to give any estimate before arrival, that's a red flag.

Confirm the destination in advance. Make sure the driver and dispatcher agree on where the vehicle is going before work begins. Disputes about destination after the fact are a common setup for inflated bills.

Know your rights. Nevada law requires towing companies operating on private property to post their rates. For emergency roadside tows, asking for the rate structure upfront is your right as a customer.

Check your insurance. Many auto insurance policies include roadside assistance coverage that reimburses towing costs up to a set amount. If yours does, ask for an itemized receipt so you can file the claim properly.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does insurance cover towing in Nevada?**

Many policies include roadside assistance or will reimburse towing up to a set amount. Check your policy's declarations page or call your insurer — it's worth knowing before you ever need a tow.

**Why did my tow cost more than the estimate?**

Common reasons include the vehicle requiring a different tow method than expected, additional mileage if the destination changed, or after-hours surcharges that weren't communicated upfront. Always ask for an itemized receipt and compare it against the original quote.

**Is flatbed always more expensive than wheel-lift?**

Yes, generally. But flatbed is also significantly safer for most modern vehicles, particularly front-wheel-drive and all-wheel-drive cars where wheel-lift towing can damage the drivetrain. The price difference is usually worth it for vehicle protection.

**Are towing rates regulated in Nevada?**

Nevada does not set a statewide maximum towing rate for private calls — rates are set by individual companies. However, companies that operate under contract with local governments or police departments may have rate schedules that are regulated by those agreements.
