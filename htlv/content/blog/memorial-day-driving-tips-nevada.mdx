Memorial Day weekend marks the unofficial start of summer in Las Vegas, and the roads reflect it. I-15 fills up with people heading to California. US-95 backs up with locals going to Lake Mead and Mount Charleston. The Strip is at near-capacity. And somewhere in all of that traffic, a car that barely made it through winter is about to have a very bad day.

We see a significant spike in roadside calls every Memorial Day weekend. Most of them are preventable. Here is what to know before you head out.

## Why This Weekend Specifically is Hard on Cars

Three things combine to create a breakdown-heavy Memorial Day weekend in Nevada every year.

The temperature jump is the first one. Temperatures often cross the 100-degree mark for the first time of the year around Memorial Day. Cars that handled spring temperatures fine can struggle when the heat suddenly spikes. Cooling systems that were marginal get pushed over the edge. Tire pressure fluctuates as the asphalt heats up. Batteries that were borderline through winter finally give out.

High mileage driving is the second factor. People are taking road trips, driving to the lake, making multiple trips across the valley. Vehicles that typically handle a short commute each day are suddenly being driven 150 to 200 miles in a day, and small issues that never had a chance to develop into full problems now do.

Deferred maintenance is the third factor. A lot of people put off oil changes, tire rotations, and general checkups through the holidays and into spring. Memorial Day weekend is when that deferred maintenance catches up to them.

## What to Check Before You Head Out

If you are planning any significant driving this weekend, run through this list the day before.

**Tires.** Check the pressure when the tires are cold, before you drive anywhere. The correct pressure is on the sticker inside your driver's door jamb. Also look at the tread — a coin inserted into the groove with Lincoln's head facing down should not show the top of his head. If it does, the tread is worn enough to think about replacing before a highway trip in summer heat.

**Coolant.** Look at the coolant overflow reservoir with the engine cold. It should be between the min and max lines. If it is at or below the minimum, top it off before you go. If it keeps needing to be topped off, that is a sign of a leak that should be looked at.

**Oil.** Check it on a level surface with a cold engine. If it is low or dark and gritty, an oil change before the weekend is worth it.

**Battery.** If your battery is more than three years old and you have not had it tested recently, stop by any auto parts store. They will test it for free in five minutes. A battery that is on its way out will not survive a full day of driving in 105-degree heat.

**Spare tire.** Check that it is inflated and that you have a jack and lug wrench in the car. Flat tire calls on holiday weekends are extremely common and response times are longer than usual because of demand.

## On the Road Tips

Leave early or leave late. The worst traffic of Memorial Day weekend in Las Vegas happens Friday afternoon and Sunday evening. If you can shift your timing by a couple of hours you will avoid the worst of it and spend less time sitting in stop-and-go traffic, which is harder on your car than highway driving.

Keep your gas tank above half. Gas stations along popular routes fill up on holiday weekends and running low at the wrong time means pulling off in an area with limited options. Running out of gas on a holiday weekend in Nevada can mean a significant wait for fuel delivery.

Watch your temperature gauge. As covered in our overheating article, the moment that gauge starts climbing above its normal position, take action. Turn off the AC, turn on the heat if necessary, and get off the highway at the next exit. Do not push through on a holiday weekend — the traffic density means response times for tow trucks are longer than a normal weekday.

Pull over if you are tired. This is not car maintenance advice but it is the one that matters most. Nevada highway fatigue accidents spike on holiday weekends. If you are drowsy, pull over, rest, and continue when you are alert. No road trip schedule is worth it.
