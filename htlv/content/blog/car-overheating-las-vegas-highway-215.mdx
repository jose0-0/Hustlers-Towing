The 215 beltway in Las Vegas is one of those roads that looks fine on a map but can become a serious problem if your car decides to overheat on it. Long stretches between exits, high speeds, temperatures that push past 110 degrees in summer, and an asphalt surface that radiates heat back up at your car's underbody. It is one of the worst places in the valley to have an engine cooling problem.

The good news is that if you catch it early and respond correctly, an overheating engine does not have to mean a destroyed engine. The bad news is that most drivers wait too long or do the wrong thing, and that is when a minor cooling issue turns into a very expensive repair.

Here is what to do the moment you notice your temperature gauge climbing.

## What the Temperature Gauge Is Actually Telling You

Most cars have a temperature gauge that sits comfortably in the middle during normal operation. When it starts climbing toward the red zone, your engine coolant is getting hotter than it should be. This can happen for several reasons — low coolant, a failing water pump, a stuck thermostat, a blown head gasket, or simply the combination of stop-and-go traffic and Las Vegas summer heat overwhelming a cooling system that is marginal to begin with.

The moment you see that gauge moving higher than normal, take it seriously. You have a window to act before real damage happens. Once the engine actually overheats and shuts down, or worse, you keep driving through an overheat warning, you are looking at potential head gasket failure, warped cylinder heads, or a seized engine — all of which cost thousands of dollars to repair or replace.

## Step One: Turn Off the AC Immediately

Your air conditioning puts a significant load on the engine. The moment you notice the temperature climbing, turn it off. This reduces the load on the cooling system and buys you time.

If the temperature is climbing fast and turning off the AC does not help, try turning the heat on full blast. It sounds miserable in July, but your heater core is essentially a second radiator that pulls heat out of the coolant and into the cabin. It can actually lower the coolant temperature enough to get you to the next exit safely.

## Step Two: Get Off the 215 at the Nearest Exit

Do not push through to your destination. Do not tell yourself it will probably be fine. Get off the freeway at the next available exit, even if it means a detour.

The 215 has some long gaps between exits in sections — particularly out near the northwest valley and the Summerlin corridor. If you are between exits, pull onto the shoulder as far right as you can, turn on your hazards, and stop the car. Running an overheating engine, even at highway speed, is worse than stopping and waiting.

## Step Three: Turn Off the Engine and Wait

Once you are off the road or on the shoulder, turn the engine off. Do not open the hood immediately. The coolant system is under pressure when it is hot, and opening a hot radiator cap can cause scalding fluid to spray out under pressure. Wait at least 15 to 20 minutes before touching anything under the hood.

While you wait, stay in the car if you are on the freeway shoulder. Put on your hazards. Call for help.

## Step Four: Do Not Add Cold Water to a Hot Engine

This is one of the most common mistakes people make. Pouring cold water into an overheated engine can crack the engine block or cylinder heads due to thermal shock. If you have coolant or water to add, wait until the engine has cooled down completely before opening the radiator cap or overflow reservoir.

## When to Call a Tow

## Why Overheating Happens More in Las Vegas

Las Vegas summer is genuinely hard on cooling systems in a way that moderate climates are not. Ambient temperatures above 110 degrees mean your radiator is trying to cool down a hot engine using outside air that is already extremely hot. The temperature differential your cooling system relies on is much smaller than it would be in most parts of the country.

Add stop-and-go traffic on the 215 during rush hour, which reduces airflow through the radiator, and you have the perfect conditions for a marginal cooling system to fail.

The best way to prevent this is to have your cooling system inspected before summer. A coolant flush and fill, a check of the water pump, thermostat, and radiator cap, and a pressure test of the system takes less than an hour and catches problems before they strand you on the freeway in July.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I drive home if the temperature went into the red but came back down?**

Technically the car may be driveable, but we would not recommend it without at least pulling over and checking the coolant level first. An engine that overheated once is more likely to do it again in the same drive. If the coolant level is low, that is your answer right there.

**How long does it take for an overheated engine to cool down enough to check?**

At least 15 to 20 minutes at minimum. Longer is safer. The system is under pressure and the temperature of the metal components takes time to drop even after you cut the engine.

**Is my engine ruined if it overheated?**

Not necessarily. A brief overheat that was caught quickly may cause no lasting damage. A sustained overheat, or one where the driver kept going after warning signs appeared, is where serious engine damage becomes likely. Have a mechanic check it before driving it again.
