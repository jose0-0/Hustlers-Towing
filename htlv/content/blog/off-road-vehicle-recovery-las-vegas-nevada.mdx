Las Vegas sits at the edge of some of the most accessible and varied off-road terrain in the country. Red Rock Canyon, the Valley of Fire, Eldorado Canyon, the McCullough Hills, Jean Dry Lake, and dozens of less well-known trails are all within an hour of the Strip. The Las Vegas off-road community is substantial, and weekend trail runs are a regular part of life for a significant number of valley residents.

When something goes wrong out there — a vehicle gets stuck in sand or loose rock, rolls a tire off a bead, gets high-centered on a ledge, or simply breaks down far from a paved road — the process for getting help is fundamentally different from a standard roadside breakdown. Here is what that process looks like and what you need to know before you head into the desert.

## What Makes Off-Road Recovery Different

A standard tow happens on or very near a paved road. The tow truck can drive to within a short distance of your vehicle, hook up or load it, and drive away. The terrain is predictable and the equipment requirements are straightforward.

Off-road recovery involves getting a vehicle out of a situation where it cannot move under its own power and where a standard tow truck either cannot reach it or cannot safely operate. The terrain may involve loose sand, steep grades, large rocks, narrow trails, or significant distance from any paved surface.

The equipment and techniques required are different. A winch capable of pulling a stuck vehicle out of sand or off a ledge. Traction boards and recovery points. High-lift jacks for vehicles that are high-centered. In some cases, a second vehicle with four-wheel drive that can get close enough to assist. For serious recoveries involving rollovers or vehicles that have gone down a steep embankment, heavy recovery equipment is required.

## What Off-Road Recovery Actually Involves

The recovery process starts with assessing the situation. A vehicle that is stuck in soft sand is a different problem from one that has rolled, gone over a berm, or is perched on a rock with no traction. The right approach depends on the specific problem and the terrain around it.

For a stuck vehicle in accessible terrain, a winch recovery from a recovery-equipped truck or a capable four-wheel drive vehicle is often the fastest option. A proper recovery point on the stuck vehicle — a rated recovery hook, a tow strap attached to a frame-mounted point — is essential. Using a bumper, a wheel, or a tow ball as a recovery attachment point is how recoveries go wrong.

For a vehicle that is significantly off-trail, on a steep slope, or in terrain that a recovery truck cannot reach, the situation may require multiple vehicles, additional personnel, and more time than a simple winch pull.

For vehicles that have rolled or sustained significant damage, the recovery overlaps with heavy equipment operations and may require specialized recovery operators.

## Before You Head Out: What Matters

The best off-road recovery is the one you do not need. A few things significantly reduce the risk of needing a recovery.

Know the trail before you go. Las Vegas-area trails vary enormously in difficulty, and trails that look accessible on a map can involve terrain that is well beyond a stock vehicle's capability. Research the specific trail, talk to people who have run it recently, and be honest about your vehicle's capability and your own experience level.

Tell someone where you are going and when you expect to be back. Cell service in much of the terrain around Las Vegas is limited to nonexistent. If something goes wrong and you have not told anyone your plans, it may be a long time before anyone comes looking.

Carry recovery gear appropriate for the terrain. At minimum, a tow strap with rated hooks, a high-lift jack, traction boards for sand and loose terrain, and enough water for a longer-than-expected stay. A portable jump starter and a tire repair kit cover the more common mechanical issues.

Know how to use your vehicle's four-wheel drive system properly before you are in a situation that requires it.
