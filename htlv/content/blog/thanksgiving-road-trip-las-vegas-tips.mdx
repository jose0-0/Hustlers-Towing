Thanksgiving week is the single busiest travel period of the year for I-15 out of Las Vegas. The freeway toward California fills up Wednesday afternoon and does not fully clear until Sunday evening. US-93 toward Arizona and US-95 toward the Pacific Northwest see similar volume. Millions of people are making the same trip at the same time, which means any car trouble you have on the road happens in the worst possible traffic conditions.

The good news is that most holiday weekend breakdowns are preventable. Here are the seven things worth checking before you leave.

## 1. Battery

This is the most important item on the list and the one most drivers skip. November in Las Vegas means cooler nights, and the drop in temperature is exactly when a battery weakened by summer heat finally fails. Your battery has had three to four months since summer to continue degrading, and a cold Thanksgiving morning departure is a common trigger for a failure that was building since August.

If your battery is more than two years old, get it tested before the trip. Free at any auto parts store, takes five minutes. If the test comes back marginal or weak, replace it before you leave rather than hoping it makes the trip.

## 2. Tires

Check the pressure on all four tires and your spare. November temperatures in Las Vegas are significantly cooler than summer, which means tire pressure has dropped since you last checked it — air contracts in cooler weather. Check pressure in the morning before the car has been driven for the most accurate reading.

Also do a quick visual inspection. Look at the tread depth, the sidewall condition, and check for anything embedded in the tire that might be causing a slow leak. A screw in a tire that has been slowly losing air for weeks can become a flat tire on I-15 near Barstow on the Wednesday before Thanksgiving.

Check the spare while you are at it. A flat spare discovered on the side of the road is a significantly worse situation than a flat spare discovered in your driveway.

## 3. Oil

If an oil change is overdue or coming up in the next few thousand miles, do it before the trip. Highway driving in cool weather is relatively easy on oil, but starting a multi-day trip with oil that is due for a change is not ideal. A fresh oil change before a long drive is a simple item to knock out in the week before you leave.

## 4. Coolant

Pop the hood and check the coolant overflow reservoir with a cold engine. It should be between the min and max lines. November temperatures in Nevada mean the cooling system is under less thermal stress than in summer, but it still needs to be at the right level. More importantly, coolant that has not been flushed in several years becomes acidic and can corrode components from the inside — if you cannot remember the last time you had a coolant flush, it is worth doing before a long trip.

## 5. Brakes

If your brakes have been making any noise — squealing when you first apply them, grinding at any point, or a pulsing feeling in the pedal — have them inspected before a highway trip. Thanksgiving travel involves a lot of heavy braking in traffic, and brakes that are marginal on your regular commute may not perform well under sustained load on I-15 with cars packed bumper to bumper.

## 6. Lights and Signals

Thanksgiving travel includes early morning departures and late evening arrivals, and November means shorter days. Make sure all your lights are working — headlights both low and high beam, taillights, brake lights, and turn signals. Walk around the car and have someone sit in the driver's seat to check the brake lights if you do not have another way to see them.

## 7. Emergency Kit and Phone Charger

Thanksgiving weekend creates the conditions where a breakdown goes from inconvenient to genuinely bad. Traffic volume means help takes longer to arrive, temperatures are cool but not dangerous for adults, and you may have kids in the car.

Make sure your emergency kit is stocked — water, jumper cables or a portable jump starter, a flashlight, basic tools, and a phone charger. On a trip with family, having a car charger and a backup battery pack for phones is especially useful if you end up waiting on the side of the road for any length of time.
