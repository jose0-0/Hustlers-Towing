One of the most common questions we hear after completing a tow job is whether the driver's insurance will cover it. The honest answer is that it depends on your specific policy, and the difference between a policy that covers towing and one that does not is sometimes a $5 to $15 monthly add-on that a lot of people never thought to check.

Here is what you need to know about towing coverage in Nevada, how to find out what your policy actually includes, and how to get reimbursed if you paid out of pocket.

## How Towing Coverage Works in Auto Insurance

Standard auto insurance policies — liability, collision, and comprehensive — do not automatically include towing and roadside assistance. These coverages address damage to your vehicle and liability for accidents, not the cost of getting a disabled vehicle to a mechanic.

Towing coverage typically comes in as a separate add-on, sometimes called roadside assistance, emergency road service, or towing and labor coverage depending on the insurer. This add-on usually costs between $5 and $15 per month and covers services like towing, jump starts, flat tire changes, lockout service, and fuel delivery up to specified limits.

Some policies include a flat dollar amount for towing reimbursement — say, up to $100 per tow — while others cover the full cost up to a mileage limit. The details vary significantly between insurers and between policy tiers.

## How to Find Out What Your Policy Includes

The fastest way is to log into your insurer's app or website and look at your coverage summary. Most major insurers — State Farm, GEICO, Progressive, Allstate, Farmers, USAA — display all your active coverages in the account dashboard. Look for anything labeled roadside assistance, towing coverage, or emergency road service.

If you cannot find it online, call your agent or the insurer's customer service line and ask directly: does my policy include roadside assistance or towing coverage, and if so, what does it cover and what are the limits?

Do this now, before something goes wrong. Trying to navigate your insurance policy from the side of the road at night is not the ideal time to discover you do not have coverage.

## What Nevada-Specific Factors to Know

Nevada does not require auto insurance policies to include towing or roadside assistance. It is purely optional, which means many drivers in the state do not have it simply because they never thought to add it.

Nevada does require minimum liability coverage for all registered vehicles, but that requirement says nothing about roadside services. If you bought the minimum required coverage and nothing more, there is a good chance you do not have towing included.

Nevada's large geographic footprint also means towing distances can be significant if you break down on a remote highway. A policy that reimburses up to $50 for towing is genuinely useful for a short intra-city tow but may cover only a fraction of the cost for a long-haul recovery from a remote stretch of US-93 or I-15 near the state line.

## How to File a Towing Reimbursement Claim

If you paid for a tow out of pocket and your policy includes towing coverage, you can typically file for reimbursement after the fact.

Save the itemized receipt from the towing company. This is the most important step. The receipt should show the company name, the services performed, the pickup location, the destination, the mileage, and the total charged. Without an itemized receipt, reimbursement claims are harder to process.

Contact your insurer and let them know you need to file a roadside assistance reimbursement claim. Most insurers have an online claim portal for this, or you can call the claims line.

Provide the receipt, your policy number, the date and location of the breakdown, and a brief description of what happened. The process is typically straightforward and does not require the same level of documentation as a collision claim.

Be aware that some insurers track roadside claims and may factor a high volume of claims into future rate decisions. For most drivers, one or two claims per year are not going to affect your rates, but it is worth knowing that the option exists to just pay out of pocket for a minor claim if you are concerned about it.

## When Your Coverage Is Not Enough

Even with towing coverage, some situations fall outside what the policy will reimburse. Long-distance tows that exceed the mileage limit, specialized equipment for exotic or heavy vehicles, and recovery from off-road situations may not be fully covered.

In those cases, a combination of insurance reimbursement for what is covered and out-of-pocket for the remainder is the typical outcome. Knowing your coverage limits before a long trip or before heading off-road helps you plan accordingly.

## The Bottom Line

Spend five minutes today checking whether your current policy includes towing coverage. If it does not, call your insurer and ask what it would cost to add roadside assistance. For most drivers in Nevada, the cost is modest and the peace of mind on long desert drives is worth it.
