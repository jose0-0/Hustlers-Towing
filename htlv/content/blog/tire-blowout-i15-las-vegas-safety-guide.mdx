A tire blowout at 70 miles per hour feels like someone sideswiped you. The car lurches, the steering wheel pulls hard, and your instincts tell you to do exactly the wrong thing.

Most blowout accidents aren't caused by the blowout itself. They're caused by how the driver reacts in the first two or three seconds. Brake hard, swerve, or lift off the gas too quickly and you can lose control completely. React correctly and you'll slow down smoothly and reach the shoulder without incident.

I-15 through Las Vegas runs through some of the most congested stretches of freeway in Nevada, with heavy truck traffic, tourist rental cars, and locals all sharing the road. Knowing what to do before it happens is the difference between a bad day and a serious accident.

## Why Blowouts Happen More Often in Nevada

Before getting into the response, it helps to understand why blowouts are more common here than in most states.

Heat is the primary factor. Asphalt surface temperatures in Las Vegas summer regularly exceed 150°F. At those temperatures, the air inside your tires expands significantly — tires that were properly inflated in the morning can become dangerously overinflated by midday. Overinflated tires have less contact with the road and are more susceptible to blowout from road debris or impact.

Underinflation is the other side of the same problem. An underinflated tire flexes more with each rotation, generating heat from the inside out. On a long stretch of hot Nevada freeway, an underinflated tire can build up enough internal heat to fail catastrophically.

Road debris is also a significant factor on I-15, which sees heavy commercial truck traffic. Retreaded truck tires shed chunks of rubber — the pieces you see scattered on the shoulder — and metal debris from loads is common. A sharp impact at highway speed can cause an immediate blowout even on a properly inflated tire in good condition.

## The First Three Seconds — What to Do

This is the part that matters most. Everything that follows is easier once you've handled the initial reaction correctly.

**Do not brake.** This is the hardest instinct to override. When something goes wrong at highway speed, every part of your brain says hit the brakes. But hard braking after a blowout can cause the vehicle to spin, especially if a front tire has blown. Keep your foot completely off the brake pedal for the first few seconds.

**Hold the steering wheel firmly with both hands.** A blowout, especially a front tire, will pull the car hard in the direction of the failed tire. Grip the wheel at the nine and three o'clock positions and resist the pull. You don't need to counter-steer aggressively — just hold your line.

**Ease onto the accelerator briefly.** This sounds completely wrong, but a slight increase in throttle helps stabilize the vehicle in the first moment after a blowout. You're not trying to speed up — you're trying to maintain forward momentum long enough for the car to stabilize before you begin slowing down.

**Let the car slow naturally.** Once the vehicle is tracking straight and you've stabilized the steering, ease off the throttle gradually and let engine braking and rolling resistance bring the speed down. The car will decelerate on its own faster than you might expect with a flat tire.

**Steer toward the shoulder.** Once you're below about 40 miles per hour and the vehicle is under control, use your turn signal and move to the right shoulder. Take the exit if one is immediately available — a side street is safer than a freeway shoulder.

## Reaching the Shoulder Safely on I-15

I-15 through Las Vegas has some challenging stretches when it comes to shoulder access. The downtown connector, the stretch through the resort corridor, and the interchange with US-95 all have sections with narrow or no shoulders and jersey barrier walls on both sides.

If you're in one of those sections when a blowout happens, your priority is reaching the nearest point where the shoulder widens — even if that means traveling further than you'd like on a flat tire. Driving on a blown tire is hard on the wheel and the vehicle, but it's significantly safer than stopping in a live lane or against a jersey barrier with no clearance.

Once you reach a safe spot on the shoulder, as far right as possible and ideally past the white line, put the car in park and get your hazards on immediately.

## What to Do Once You're on the Shoulder

Do not attempt to change the tire yourself on a freeway shoulder. This cannot be said strongly enough. Tire changes on I-15 or any other Las Vegas freeway put you inches from traffic moving at 65 to 75 miles per hour. Every year in Nevada, people are struck and killed by passing vehicles while working on disabled cars on freeway shoulders. The risk is not worth it.

Turn on your hazard lights. If you have reflective triangles or road flares in your vehicle, place them behind the car at intervals of 50 to 100 feet. Stay in the car if possible — a stationary vehicle, even on the shoulder, provides more protection than standing outside.

## Preventing the Next One

A blowout is rarely completely random. Most of them are preceded by warning signs that got missed or ignored.

Check your tire pressure monthly. The recommended pressure is printed on a sticker inside your driver's door jamb — not on the tire itself. In Las Vegas, check it in the morning before the car has been driven, when the tires are cold, for the most accurate reading.

Inspect your tires visually every few weeks. Look for uneven wear patterns, which can indicate alignment or suspension issues. Look for bulges or bubbles in the sidewall, which are signs of internal damage and mean the tire needs to be replaced immediately. Look for embedded objects — a screw or nail in a tire often causes a slow leak rather than an immediate blowout, and catching it early means a simple plug repair instead of a blowout on the freeway.

Replace tires proactively. Most tires have a service life of four to six years regardless of tread depth. Heat accelerates rubber degradation, and Nevada's climate is hard on tires. If your tires are more than five years old, have them inspected even if the tread looks fine.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What's the difference between a blowout and a flat tire?**

A flat tire loses air gradually — you'll notice handling becoming sluggish and the car pulling to one side over a period of minutes or miles. A blowout is a rapid, catastrophic loss of air pressure — it happens in an instant and the handling effect is immediate and dramatic. The response is different: a flat can often be driven slowly to a safer location, while a blowout requires immediate stabilization of the vehicle.

**Should I use my spare tire on the freeway?**

If you're in a safe location off the freeway, a spare tire change is a reasonable option. On a freeway shoulder, we don't recommend it for the reasons described above. Call us instead.

**My tire pressure light came on. Is a blowout imminent?**

Not necessarily. The TPMS light comes on when any tire is 25 percent or more below the recommended pressure. That's low enough to affect handling and fuel economy and to increase blowout risk, but it's not an emergency in most cases. Pull over safely as soon as you can, check all four tires, and inflate or repair as needed. Don't ignore it.

**I heard a loud bang but the car seems fine. What happened?**

You likely ran over debris that caused a loud impact. Check your tire pressure and inspect all four tires visually as soon as it's safe to pull over. Sometimes an impact that sounds catastrophic does no damage; other times it causes a slow leak that won't be obvious until the tire is significantly low.
