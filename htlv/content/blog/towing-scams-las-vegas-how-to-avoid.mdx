Las Vegas has a towing problem. Not all towing companies — most operators in the valley are legitimate businesses running honest operations. But the city's combination of high tourist traffic, 24-hour activity, and a high volume of stressed drivers who just want the situation resolved creates conditions where predatory operators can and do take advantage.

Knowing how the most common scams work is the best protection against them. Here is what to watch for.

## The Bait and Switch on Pricing

This is the most common scam in Las Vegas towing. You call a number, get a quote that sounds reasonable — often something like $45 or $55 — and agree to the dispatch. When the truck arrives, the driver presents a bill that is two or three times that amount, with a list of fees that were never mentioned on the call.

The setup works because once the truck is already hooked up to your car, your negotiating position is weak. Some drivers will threaten storage fees or refuse to lower the vehicle until you pay. Others will simply act confused and claim the dispatcher must have made a mistake.

How to protect yourself: get the complete rate structure before you confirm the dispatch. That means the hook-up fee, the per-mile rate, and whether any surcharges apply. If the person on the phone cannot or will not give you those numbers, hang up and call someone else. A company operating honestly has no reason to be vague about pricing before the truck leaves the garage.

## The Predatory Accident Scene Tow

This one targets drivers who have just been in an accident and are disoriented, dealing with injuries, or focused on exchanging information with the other driver. Tow trucks sometimes monitor police scanners and show up to accident scenes before anyone has called for a tow.

The driver will offer to take your car, sometimes with significant pressure and urgency about getting it off the road immediately. In the stress of the moment, people agree without asking about the company, the rate, or where the car is being taken.

The car ends up at a storage lot you did not choose, and you find out later that storage fees have been accruing at a rate that makes recovery expensive.

How to protect yourself: you have the right to choose your own towing company at an accident scene. Law enforcement may call a tow if your car is blocking traffic and cannot be moved, but even then you can often request a specific company. If you are not injured and can handle the situation, call your preferred company directly rather than accepting an unsolicited offer from a driver who appeared on their own.

## The Fake Dispatch Broker

You search for towing in Las Vegas, call what looks like a local number, and talk to someone who sounds helpful and gives you a quick quote and a short ETA. What you do not know is that you called a national call center that is now calling out to find any available contractor to take your job.

The contractor who shows up may have no connection to the company you thought you called, may charge differently than what was quoted, and may have different standards of service and equipment. When something goes wrong, you have limited recourse because the company you called does not actually own the truck or employ the driver.

How to protect yourself: ask directly whether the company owns its trucks and employs its drivers. Ask the name of the driver who will be coming and what truck they are in. A direct operator can answer these questions specifically. A broker cannot.

## The Unnecessary Tow

Your car has broken down and a passing tow truck stops to offer help. The driver looks at the car, shakes their head, and tells you it needs to be towed — even though the actual problem is something simple like a dead battery that could be jump-started on the spot.

An unnecessary tow means a towing fee plus mileage plus potentially storage, for a situation that a jump start or a fuel delivery would have resolved for a fraction of the cost.

How to protect yourself: if a driver who stopped unsolicited is recommending a tow, get a second opinion before agreeing. Call a company you have researched and describe the symptoms. A reputable company will tell you honestly whether a jump start, fuel delivery, or roadside service might resolve the problem before recommending a full tow.

## The Inflated Mileage Charge

You get a bill that seems high for a short local tow. When you check the mileage listed on the invoice against the actual distance in Google Maps, the numbers do not match. Some operators charge mileage from their garage location rather than from your pickup point, or simply inflate the mileage on the invoice.

How to protect yourself: check the mileage on any towing invoice against the actual point-to-point distance of your tow. If the numbers do not align, ask for an explanation before paying. The mileage charged should reflect the distance from where your car was picked up to where it was delivered.

## What to Do if You Have Already Been Scammed

If you believe you were overcharged or misled by a towing company in Las Vegas, you have options.

File a complaint with the Nevada Transportation Authority, which licenses and regulates towing companies in the state. They investigate billing complaints and have the authority to take action against companies that violate regulations.

Dispute the charge with your credit card company if you paid by card, providing documentation of what was quoted versus what was charged.

Leave a detailed review on Google describing specifically what happened. This protects other drivers and creates accountability.

Contact the Better Business Bureau for Las Vegas if the company is registered there.

The most important thing is to document everything. Save the phone number you called, note the time and date, keep any text communications, and photograph the invoice.
