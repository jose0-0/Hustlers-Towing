Las Vegas has a large and active motorcycle community. The weather is rideable for most of the year, the roads around the valley offer everything from freeway commuting to canyon runs in the Spring Mountains, and the culture around bikes is strong. When something goes wrong on a ride — a mechanical failure, a flat tire, a dead battery, or an accident — the process for getting help is different from a car breakdown in ways that every rider should understand.

## Why Motorcycle Towing Is Different

A motorcycle cannot simply be hooked up to a tow truck the way a car can. There is no standard wheel-lift or flatbed loading approach that works without additional equipment and care. The bike needs to be secured upright, or loaded in a way that accounts for its shape, center of gravity, and fragile components, in a way that a car does not require.

The wrong approach to motorcycle towing causes real damage. A bike that is strapped incorrectly can have its fairings cracked, its frame stressed, or its finish damaged by contact with strap hardware. A bike that is not properly secured on a flatbed can tip during transport, which causes significantly more damage than the original problem that required the tow.

## What Proper Motorcycle Towing Looks Like

A motorcycle tow should use a flatbed truck equipped with a wheel chock — a front wheel cradle that holds the bike upright and prevents lateral movement. The chock locks the front wheel in place so the bike stays vertical during loading, transport, and unloading.

Straps are applied at the handlebars or front forks — not across painted bodywork, chrome, or fragile components. Soft loops or cloth-covered strap hooks are used at contact points to prevent scratching. The bike should be strapped firmly enough that it does not shift during transport, but not so tight that it stresses the suspension or frame.

A driver who does not have experience with motorcycles may not know any of this. This is why it is worth asking specifically when you call — does the driver have experience with motorcycle towing, and does the truck have a wheel chock? A company that regularly handles motorcycles will answer without hesitation.

## Common Reasons Riders Call for a Tow

Dead batteries are one of the most common motorcycle roadside calls in Las Vegas. Motorcycles have smaller batteries than cars, and those smaller batteries degrade faster in the desert heat. A bike that was starting fine in spring may not make it through a Las Vegas summer without battery issues.

Flat tires are common and present a different problem than a car flat because most motorcycles do not carry a spare. Tubeless tires can sometimes be plugged roadside with a portable repair kit, but tube-type tires and sidewall damage require a tow to a shop.

Mechanical failures — clutch cable snapping, fuel delivery issues, overheating, chain problems — happen and are generally not fixable roadside. A tow to your mechanic is the practical outcome.

After an accident, even a minor low-side that does not result in injury, the bike should be inspected before being ridden further. A tow to a shop for assessment is the right call even if the bike appears to be running.

## What to Do When You Break Down on a Ride

Get the bike off the road if it can still be moved. Motorcycles are less visible than cars to passing traffic, which makes a bike stopped on a shoulder or in a lane more dangerous than a disabled car in the same position.

If the bike cannot be moved, get yourself away from traffic first. A rider standing next to a motorcycle on a freeway shoulder is in a genuinely dangerous position. Get to safety and then deal with the bike.

## What to Have With You on a Ride

A few items worth carrying on any significant ride in or around Las Vegas.

A portable tire plug kit for tubeless tires. It will not fix every flat but it handles the most common puncture scenarios and can get you moving without a tow.

A portable jump starter sized for a motorcycle. Motorcycle-specific jump starters are compact and lightweight enough to carry in a saddlebag or tank bag, and they handle the relatively small battery in a motorcycle easily.

Your roadside assistance information and the number of a towing company you have vetted. Not every roadside assistance program handles motorcycles well — check whether your plan specifically covers motorcycles before you need it.

## Cruisers, Sport Bikes, and Adventure Bikes

Different styles of motorcycle have different towing considerations. Cruisers with lower ground clearance and wide handlebars may require specific chock positioning. Sport bikes with full fairing need careful strap placement to avoid contact with painted surfaces. Adventure bikes with panniers and luggage systems need the loaded weight accounted for in how the bike is balanced and strapped.

When you call, mention the style of bike and any aftermarket additions — fairings, luggage, crash bars — so the driver knows what they are working with before they arrive.
