Most people have never watched a tow truck load and secure their vehicle, so it's natural to wonder what actually happens to your car once you hand over the keys and the truck pulls away. The honest answer is that a properly executed tow should leave your car in exactly the same condition it was in when the driver arrived. The key word is properly.

Here's what a correct tow looks like, what can cause damage, and what you can do to protect yourself.

## What a Good Tow Looks Like

On a flatbed tow, the driver will tilt the bed down to road level and drive or winch your vehicle up onto the platform. Once the car is positioned correctly on the bed, it gets secured with wheel straps, one around each tire, and safety chains as a secondary attachment. The straps hold the tires down and prevent any movement during transport. The vehicle should not shift, rock, or roll at any point during the trip.

On a wheel-lift tow, the driver positions the yoke under two wheels, typically the front ones, and lifts that end of the car. The other two wheels ride on the road. The vehicle should be centered on the yoke and the suspension components should not be under unusual stress during a short, correctly executed wheel-lift tow.

When you arrive at the destination, the driver should lower the vehicle carefully and pull the straps before releasing the car to you or to the shop. The whole process should feel deliberate, not rushed.

## What Can Cause Damage

Most towing damage comes down to three things: the wrong method for the vehicle, careless loading, or improper securing.

Using a wheel-lift method on an all-wheel-drive vehicle is probably the most common source of drivetrain damage. When an AWD vehicle is towed with two wheels rolling on the ground, the rotation feeds through the drivetrain to components that are not designed to operate without engine lubrication. Short wheel-lift tows on AWD vehicles usually cause no immediate problem, but longer tows or vehicles with already-stressed drivetrains can sustain transmission or transfer case damage.

Careless winching can damage bumper covers, undercarriage components, and paint if the driver uses the wrong attachment points or doesn't position the cable correctly. The winch cable or strap should attach to dedicated tow points on the vehicle, not to bumper trim or decorative components.

Improper wheel strap placement can damage lower body panels, wheel wells, or even the wheels themselves if the straps are too tight in the wrong position.

## How to Protect Yourself

A few simple steps make a real difference.

Be present when your vehicle is loaded if at all possible. Watch the driver work. A professional driver doing a careful job has nothing to hide and no reason to mind being watched. If something looks rushed or wrong, say something before the truck leaves, not after.

Take photos of your car before the driver arrives if you can. A quick walkthrough with your phone camera, documenting any pre-existing damage, means there's no question about what was there before versus what might have happened during transport.

Tell the driver about anything specific to your vehicle. Low front bumper, wider-than-standard wheels, recent body work, an aftermarket exhaust, a broken piece of trim that's hanging loose. Anything the driver should know about before loading, tell them.

Confirm the tow method before the driver starts. If your vehicle is AWD, make sure the driver knows this and is sending a flatbed. If you're not sure what kind of truck is coming, ask when you call.

Get the driver's name and a receipt before you leave. The receipt should show the company name, the driver's name, the pickup location, the destination, and the services charged. If there's any dispute about damage later, you want a clear paper trail.

## What to Do If Your Car Is Damaged During a Tow

Document it immediately. Take photos at the destination before the truck leaves. Note the damage on the receipt if the driver is present, or photograph the receipt alongside the damaged area.

Contact the towing company directly with the documentation. A reputable company will take damage claims seriously and have a process for handling them. If the company is unresponsive or dismissive, you can escalate to the Nevada Transportation Authority, which licenses towing companies in the state, or to your insurance company if you have comprehensive coverage.

The important thing is to not wait. The time to document and report damage is the same day, ideally before the tow truck driver leaves the scene.
