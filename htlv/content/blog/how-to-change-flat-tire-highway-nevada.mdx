Getting a flat tire on the highway is stressful, but it's manageable if you know what you're doing. The tricky part is that changing a tire on the side of a busy Nevada freeway is actually one of the more dangerous things a regular driver can do. Every year people get hit by passing vehicles while working on their cars on the shoulder.

So before we get into the steps, let's be clear about something: if you're on I-15, US-95, the 215, or any other high-speed freeway, calling a tow truck is genuinely the safer option. The steps below are for when you're on a surface street, a quiet road, or somewhere you can get safely clear of traffic.

If you're on a fast freeway, scroll to the bottom and call us instead.

## Before You Even Think About Changing the Tire

Getting to a safe location is more important than getting the tire changed quickly.

If you feel the tire go flat while driving, don't stop immediately in the traffic lane. Turn on your hazard lights and steer gradually toward the right shoulder. If there's an exit ramp nearby, take it. A surface street parking lot or quiet side street is a much better place to change a tire than a freeway shoulder.

Once you've stopped, pull as far to the right as possible. You want as much distance between you and moving traffic as you can get. Put the car in park, turn off the engine, and apply the parking brake. If you have wheel wedges or large rocks nearby, place them against the tires that won't be lifted to prevent the car from rolling.

Set out your reflective triangles or road flares behind the vehicle before you start working. You want other drivers to see you from a distance.

## What You Need

Most vehicles come with a spare tire, a jack, and a lug wrench in the trunk or under the cargo floor. If you've never looked for yours, find it before you need it. The owner's manual will tell you exactly where it is and how the jack is set up for your specific vehicle.

You'll also want a flashlight if it's dark, a pair of gloves, and something to kneel on if the ground is hot. Nevada asphalt in summer can be hot enough to burn your knees through jeans.

## Step by Step

**Loosen the lug nuts before you jack the car up.** This is the step most people get backwards. If you try to loosen the lug nuts after the wheel is in the air, the tire will just spin. While the car is still on the ground, use the lug wrench to break the lug nuts loose by turning them counterclockwise. You don't need to remove them yet, just crack the resistance.

**Find the correct jack point.** This is important. Placing the jack in the wrong spot can damage the vehicle's frame or rocker panels. The correct jack points are usually reinforced metal areas just behind the front wheels and just ahead of the rear wheels. They often have a small notch or marking. Your owner's manual will show exactly where they are for your vehicle.

**Raise the vehicle.** Position the jack at the correct point and raise the vehicle until the flat tire is about six inches off the ground. Never get under a vehicle supported only by a floor jack.

**Remove the lug nuts and the flat tire.** Finish removing the lug nuts and put them somewhere you won't lose them, like your pocket. Pull the flat tire straight toward you and set it aside.

**Mount the spare.** Lift the spare tire onto the wheel bolts and push it flush against the hub. Hand tighten the lug nuts in a star or cross pattern, not in a circle. This keeps the wheel seated evenly.

**Lower the vehicle and finish tightening.** Lower the jack until the tire contacts the ground but the vehicle weight isn't fully on it yet. Tighten the lug nuts firmly in the same star pattern, then lower the rest of the way and remove the jack. Give the lug nuts a final tighten with the vehicle on the ground.

**Check the spare's pressure before driving.** If you have a gauge, check it. Many spare tires, especially compact spare "donuts," need to be at a specific pressure and have a speed limit, usually 50 mph max. Read the markings on the spare before you drive.

## A Few Things Worth Knowing

If your spare is a compact donut spare, it's meant to get you to a tire shop, not to drive on for days. Get the flat repaired or replaced as soon as possible.

Don't forget to get your flat tire repaired or replaced. It's easy to drive on the spare for a week and forget that you're now without a backup if something else happens.

Have your lug nuts re-torqued at a shop after driving about 50 miles on the new tire. The nuts can loosen slightly after the wheel seats fully.

You're on a busy freeway shoulder with traffic at highway speed. The risk to your safety is not worth it. You don't have a functional spare, or the spare is also flat.
