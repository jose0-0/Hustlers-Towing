Most roadside emergency kits you can buy at Walmart or AutoZone were designed for generic conditions — a cold snap in the Midwest, a rainy night in the Pacific Northwest. They come with a mylar blanket, a few jumper cables, and maybe a poncho.

Nevada drivers need something different.

When your car dies on the 215 in July, the temperature on the asphalt can hit 160°F. The nearest shade may be half a mile away. The nearest exit might be two miles back. A generic emergency kit isn't going to get you through that situation — but the right one will.

Here are the 12 items that actually belong in a Nevada driver's emergency kit, and why each one matters specifically in this climate.

## 1. Water — More Than You Think You Need

This is the non-negotiable. Every other item on this list is secondary to having enough water in your vehicle.

The standard recommendation is one gallon per person per day. In Las Vegas summer heat, that number is conservative. If you're stuck waiting for a tow truck for an hour in 115°F heat with no shade, you will go through water faster than you expect — especially if you're stressed, which you will be.

Keep at least two gallons in your vehicle during summer months. Sealed water bottles hold up better than jugs in the heat and are easier to manage. Rotate your stock every few months because water stored in plastic in extreme heat can develop an unpleasant taste and the plastic can degrade over time.

## 2. Jumper Cables or a Jump Starter Pack

Dead batteries are the single most common roadside breakdown in Las Vegas, and the heat is the reason. High temperatures accelerate battery degradation significantly — a battery that might last five years in a moderate climate may only last two or three in the Nevada desert.

Traditional jumper cables work fine if another driver stops to help. A portable jump starter pack works without needing another vehicle, which is the better option if you break down somewhere isolated or at an hour when traffic is light. Modern lithium jump starter packs are compact, affordable, and can jump a full-size V8 multiple times on a single charge.

## 3. Reflective Warning Triangles or Road Flares

Visibility is what keeps you safe while you wait for help. Road flares are effective but burn out and can't be reused. Reflective LED warning triangles are reusable, highly visible in both day and night conditions, and don't create a fire risk — which matters in Nevada where roadside vegetation can be dry enough to ignite.

Place them at 50-foot intervals behind your vehicle to give approaching drivers time to move over. Three triangles is the standard.

## 4. A Quality Flashlight with Extra Batteries

Your phone flashlight will drain your battery. A dedicated flashlight with fresh batteries is the right tool for looking under the hood, signaling for help, or navigating safely away from the vehicle at night.

LED flashlights are significantly better than older incandescent ones for this purpose — brighter, longer battery life, and more durable. Get one that takes AA or AAA batteries rather than a specialty rechargeable so you can always find a replacement.

## 5. A First Aid Kit

Basic cuts, scrapes, and minor injuries happen during breakdowns — getting out of a vehicle, moving around in the dark, handling equipment. A standard first aid kit covering bandages, antiseptic wipes, gauze, and pain reliever covers most situations.

In extreme heat, also include oral rehydration salts, which help recover from early dehydration faster than water alone.

## 6. A Basic Tool Kit

You don't need a full mechanic's toolbox. A compact kit with a few flathead and Phillips screwdrivers, a pair of pliers, an adjustable wrench, and a tire pressure gauge covers the most common minor issues — a loose battery terminal, a disconnected hose clamp, a minor fluid check.

Tire pressure matters especially in Nevada. Heat causes tire pressure to rise, and under-inflated tires are significantly more likely to blowout in high temperatures. A gauge that you can actually use takes five seconds.

## 7. Fix-a-Flat or a Portable Tire Inflator

A full spare tire and jack is the correct solution for a flat, but not every vehicle comes with a full spare anymore and not every driver is comfortable doing a tire change safely on the side of the road — especially on a highway. Fix-a-Flat can get you far enough to reach a tire shop for a minor puncture. A portable 12V tire inflator is useful for slow leaks that need topping off.

Neither of these replaces a proper tire repair, but they can get you off a dangerous stretch of highway to somewhere safer.

## 8. Emergency Mylar Blankets

Yes, these come in generic kits — but they earn their spot in a Nevada kit for a different reason than cold weather. Mylar blankets reflect radiant heat. If you break down and have to wait outside in the sun, a mylar blanket rigged as a shade cover can make a significant temperature difference. They're also useful at night when desert temperatures drop unexpectedly after a hot day.

## 9. Sunscreen and a Hat

Practical, not glamorous. If you're working on your vehicle or waiting outside on a Nevada summer day, sunburn becomes a real problem within 20 minutes. SPF 50 in a small tube and a brimmed hat take up almost no space and can prevent a miserable situation from becoming worse.

## 10. A Phone Charger and a Backup Battery Pack

Your phone is your lifeline during a breakdown — GPS, towing company phone numbers, emergency contacts. A dead phone during a breakdown is one of the most preventable problems there is.

Keep a car charger plugged in at all times and carry a backup battery pack that you keep charged. If your car is dead, the car charger doesn't help — that's when the battery pack matters.

## 11. Cash

Towing companies take cards, but there are enough situations — a compromised chip, a spotty connection in a remote area, a card that gets flagged for an unusual transaction — where having $100 to $200 in cash in your emergency kit is genuinely useful. Not for the tow itself necessarily, but for incidentals: a ride share, a meal while you wait, a simple repair at a gas station.

## 12. A Written List of Emergency Numbers

When you're stressed and your phone is low on battery or dead, having a physical list of numbers is worth more than any app. The list should include roadside assistance, a trusted mechanic, your insurance company's claims line, and a family member or friend who can help coordinate.

Keep it laminated or in a plastic sleeve so it holds up in a hot glove compartment.

## Building Your Kit

You don't need to buy a pre-packaged kit. A small plastic bin or a soft duffel bag works fine. Build it yourself using this list and you'll have exactly what you need without the filler items that come in generic kits.

Store it in your trunk or cargo area where it's accessible but not taking up cabin space. Check it twice a year — when you rotate your clocks for daylight saving time is a good trigger — to replace used items and make sure batteries are still charged.
