Las Vegas is one of the best road trip launch points in the country. Within four hours you can reach Zion National Park, the Grand Canyon's South Rim, Death Valley, Joshua Tree, the California coast, and half a dozen other destinations that people fly across the country to visit. Spring — roughly March through May — is the sweet spot: the desert heat hasn't arrived yet, the mountain passes are clear, and the national parks are at their most accessible.

It's also the time of year when we get the most calls from people broken down on remote Nevada and Utah highways with no cell service and no plan.

Here's how to make sure that's not you.

## Pre-Trip Car Check — Do This Before You Leave

A breakdown in Las Vegas is inconvenient. A breakdown 60 miles outside of St. George, Utah on US-89 is a completely different situation. Remote highway breakdowns involve longer wait times, potential cell service gaps, and fewer options for getting help quickly. The investment in a pre-trip inspection is worth it every time.

**Battery.** This is the highest-probability failure point for spring trips out of Las Vegas. Your battery has just been through a Nevada winter after surviving a Nevada summer. If it's more than three years old, have it tested at any auto parts store before you go. A battery test is free and takes five minutes. Replacing a marginal battery before a trip costs a fraction of what a roadside call and tow costs in a remote area.

**Tires.** Check all four tires including the spare. Verify the pressure — cold inflation, before the car has been driven — matches the specification on your driver's door jamb. Inspect the tread depth with a quarter: if Lincoln's head is fully visible when inserted into the tread, the tire is worn and should be replaced before a highway trip. Check the sidewalls for cracking, bubbling, or damage. Make sure your spare is inflated and that you have the jack and lug wrench.

**Fluids.** Check engine oil, coolant, brake fluid, power steering fluid, and windshield washer fluid. Low coolant going into desert driving is particularly dangerous — Death Valley and the valleys around it can see temperatures exceeding 100°F in late spring, and an engine that's marginal on coolant will overheat under those conditions.

**Brakes.** If you're heading to Zion or the Grand Canyon, you're going to be doing mountain driving with significant descents. Worn brakes that are fine for Las Vegas surface streets can fade dangerously on long downhill stretches. If your brakes have been making noise or the pedal feels soft, get them checked before you go.

**Belts and hoses.** A visual inspection of the serpentine belt and radiator hoses can catch obvious wear. A cracked belt or a bulging hose can fail at highway speed and leave you stranded with no charging system or no coolant circulation.

## Know Your Route Before You Leave

Las Vegas is surrounded by some of the most remote driving in the lower 48 states. Several of the most popular road trip routes from Las Vegas pass through areas with limited or no cell service.

US-93 toward the Hoover Dam and into Arizona passes through long stretches with minimal services. Nevada Route 160 toward Pahrump has limited coverage in some stretches. US-89 through the Arizona Strip between Las Vegas and Zion is scenic and remote in equal measure. Death Valley National Park has essentially no cell service in most of the park interior.

Before leaving, download offline maps for your entire route. Save the numbers you might need — roadside assistance, your destination's address, a family member — as physical contacts in your phone so you don't need data to find them. Know where the nearest towns are along your route so you have reference points if something goes wrong.

## What to Do If You Break Down in a Remote Area

A breakdown in a remote area requires a slightly different response than a breakdown in the Las Vegas valley.

Get completely off the road if possible. On two-lane highways like those in southern Utah and rural Nevada, the shoulder may be narrow and vehicles may be traveling at 65 to 75 miles per hour with less margin for a disabled vehicle than a multi-lane freeway. Get as far off the pavement as you safely can.

Conserve your phone battery immediately. Turn off wifi, Bluetooth, and any running apps. Lower screen brightness. You may need that battery to make a call when you reach a point with service.

If you have no cell service, stay with the vehicle. Walking in desert heat to find service is dangerous and rarely faster than waiting for another motorist to pass and either offer help or report your location. Your vehicle is visible from the road; you walking along the shoulder may not be.

Use your emergency kit. The items we covered in our roadside kit article apply directly here — water, reflective triangles, a flashlight, shade. Spring desert temperatures can reach 90°F or higher even before summer officially begins.

If you get a signal, even one bar, call or text before you lose it again. Text messages sometimes go through on weak signals when voice calls don't.

## Towing Coverage on Remote Routes

Our primary service area is Las Vegas and the surrounding valley. For breakdowns within roughly 50 to 60 miles of Las Vegas — which covers a significant portion of the Nevada side of the most popular road trip routes — we can reach you.

For breakdowns further out, particularly in Utah or Arizona, you'll need to contact a towing company in the nearest town. Knowing the nearest town on your route before you leave means you're not trying to figure that out on the side of the road with a dying phone.

For longer trips, roadside assistance coverage through your auto insurance or a standalone plan like AAA is worth considering. Many policies have towing coverage with mileage limits — check yours before you go.

## Coming Back to Las Vegas

The return trip catches people off guard. You've been driving for several hours, it's late, you're tired, and your car has been working hard. The most common return-trip issues are overheating from sustained highway driving in warm weather, tire pressure changes from the heat of the road, and battery drain from extended accessory use.

When you return to Las Vegas and get back on I-15, you're back in our coverage area. If anything feels off with the vehicle on the drive back, trust your instincts and pull over rather than pushing through to get home.

## Quick Pre-Trip Checklist

Save this before you leave.

- Battery tested if more than 3 years old
- All four tires inflated and inspected including spare
- Engine oil, coolant, brake fluid checked
- Brakes inspected if any recent noise or soft pedal
- Offline maps downloaded for full route
- Emergency numbers saved as contacts
- Roadside kit in the vehicle — water, triangles, flashlight, first aid
- Know the nearest towns along your route
