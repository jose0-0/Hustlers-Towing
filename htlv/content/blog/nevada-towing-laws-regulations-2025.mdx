Most drivers don't think about towing laws until they're in a situation where they matter — either their car has been towed from private property without warning, or they've received a bill that doesn't seem right, or they're trying to get their vehicle back and running into resistance.

Nevada has a specific set of laws governing how towing companies can operate, what they're required to tell you, and what your rights are as a vehicle owner. This isn't legal advice — it's a plain-language breakdown of the regulatory framework so you know what to expect.

## Two Types of Towing: Consent and Non-Consent

The most important distinction in Nevada towing law is between consent tows and non-consent tows.

A consent tow is one you initiate. You call a towing company, you ask them to move your vehicle, and you agree to the service. This is the kind of tow that happens when you break down and call for help. In a consent tow, the towing company is working for you.

A non-consent tow is one initiated by someone other than the vehicle owner — typically a property owner, a property manager, or law enforcement. This is what happens when your car gets towed from a private parking lot while you were shopping somewhere else, or when law enforcement orders your vehicle towed from the roadway. Nevada has specific rules governing both types, and the protections for vehicle owners are stronger in the non-consent category.

## Private Property Towing — What Property Owners Can and Cannot Do

Nevada law gives property owners the right to tow vehicles parked without authorization on their property, but there are requirements they must meet before and after doing so.

**Signage requirements.** A property must have clearly visible signage indicating that unauthorized vehicles will be towed. The signs must be posted at all entrances to the property and must specify the towing company that will be used, along with a phone number where the owner can recover their vehicle. Signs that are missing, illegible, or that don't include this information may not satisfy the legal requirement.

**Notification requirements.** In some circumstances, Nevada law requires that the vehicle owner be notified before towing occurs, or that law enforcement be notified within a specific time window after the tow is initiated.

**Vehicle release.** A towing company holding a non-consent tow vehicle must release it to the owner upon payment of fees. They cannot hold your vehicle as leverage in a dispute about whether the tow was legal — that's a separate matter for civil court. If you pay to recover your vehicle, that doesn't mean you've accepted that the tow was valid.

## Rate Transparency Requirements

Nevada requires towing companies to post their rates publicly. For non-consent tows specifically, the towing company must provide an itemized invoice showing all charges. If you receive a bill that doesn't break down what you're being charged for, you can request that itemization.

Consent tow companies operating in Las Vegas are expected to provide rate information when asked. You have the right to ask for the hook-up fee and per-mile rate before authorizing a tow, and a reputable company will give you those numbers without hesitation.

## Storage Fees — What's Regulated

Storage fees are one of the most common points of dispute in towing. Nevada law establishes some parameters around storage, including requirements for how quickly a towing company must notify the vehicle owner after a non-consent tow so that storage fees don't accumulate unnecessarily.

After a non-consent tow, the towing company is typically required to notify local law enforcement, which creates a record that the vehicle owner can search if their car goes missing. The vehicle owner then has the right to recover their vehicle as soon as fees are paid.

Storage fees are charged per day in most cases. The key point: they start accumulating from the time of the tow, not from when you find out about it. If you think your vehicle may have been towed — from a private lot, after an accident, or by law enforcement — check immediately rather than waiting.

## Law Enforcement Tows

When law enforcement orders a vehicle towed — typically because it's blocking traffic, involved in an accident, or the driver has been arrested — the vehicle goes to a tow yard that holds a contract with the relevant agency. These contracted tows have rate schedules that are negotiated with the government agency, which means they may be different from what a private towing company charges.

To find out where a law enforcement tow has taken your vehicle, contact the law enforcement agency that ordered the tow. In Las Vegas, that would be the Las Vegas Metropolitan Police Department or Nevada Highway Patrol depending on where the incident occurred. Both agencies maintain vehicle location systems that can tell you where your car is.

## Your Rights When Recovering a Vehicle

When you go to recover a towed vehicle, you have specific rights under Nevada law.

You have the right to an itemized bill before paying. You have the right to inspect your vehicle before paying to confirm it was not damaged during towing. You have the right to a receipt. You have the right to pay with a credit or debit card — towing companies in Nevada cannot require cash-only payment.

If your vehicle was damaged during a non-consent tow, document the damage with photographs before leaving the lot. The time to dispute damage is before you drive away, not a week later.

## What to Do If You Believe a Tow Was Illegal

If you believe your vehicle was towed improperly — the signage wasn't present, the fees are excessive, or you weren't properly notified — you have several options.

You can file a complaint with the Nevada Transportation Authority, which oversees towing company licensing. You can contact the Better Business Bureau. You can consult with an attorney about a civil claim if the damages are significant.

The important thing to understand is that paying to recover your vehicle does not legally waive your right to dispute the tow later. Keep your receipt, document everything, and address the dispute through the appropriate channels rather than refusing to pay on the spot — that will only extend how long you're without your vehicle.

## Consent Tows — What to Know as a Customer

For the kind of towing you initiate yourself, your main protections are around transparency and authorization. Specifically:

You have the right to know the rate before authorizing the tow. You have the right to choose where your vehicle goes. You should receive an itemized receipt. The towing company cannot charge you for services you didn't authorize.
