Your car won't start, or it died while you were driving. Before you call anyone, it helps to have a rough idea of whether you're dealing with a dead battery or a failing alternator. The reason it matters: a dead battery can often be jump-started and driven to a shop. An alternator failure usually can't, because the alternator is what keeps the battery charged while you drive. If the alternator is gone, a jump start will get you moving for a few minutes before the battery drains again and the car dies on the road.

Here's how to tell which one you're dealing with.

## What Each One Does

The battery starts the car. It provides the burst of power needed to crank the engine and get everything running. Once the engine is running, the battery's main job is done.

The alternator takes over from there. It generates electricity while the engine runs and uses that electricity to recharge the battery and power all the electrical systems in the car. Lights, radio, air conditioning, power windows, charging ports — all of that runs off the alternator while the engine is on.

When the battery dies, you can't start the car. When the alternator fails, the car might start fine but everything gradually loses power as the battery drains with nothing recharging it.

## Signs It's the Battery

The most common sign of a dead or dying battery is slow, sluggish cranking when you turn the key. You'll hear the engine trying to turn over but doing it slowly, like it doesn't quite have enough energy. Sometimes it won't crank at all and you'll hear rapid clicking instead, which means the battery is so low it can't even engage the starter properly.

Other signs the battery is the issue include the car dying after sitting overnight or after a few cold mornings in a row, all the electrical accessories going dead at the same time the car won't start, and a battery warning light that appeared recently.

In Las Vegas, battery failures are especially common in the fall and early spring. Summer heat degrades batteries fast, and the ones that barely made it through summer tend to fail when the first cold nights arrive or when the seasonal temperature swing puts extra demand on a weakened battery.

## Signs It's the Alternator

The alternator usually gives you more warning before it fails completely. Watch for these signs.

Dimming headlights while you're driving, especially at low speeds or when you're sitting at a red light. Electrical accessories like windows, seat adjusters, or the radio acting sluggish or erratic. A battery warning light that comes on while the car is running and driving fine. A whining or grinding noise from the engine bay that changes with engine RPM.

If the alternator fails completely while you're driving, here's what usually happens. The battery warning light comes on, then the car starts losing power to electrical systems progressively. The radio cuts out, the lights dim, the dashboard gauges start behaving strangely. Eventually the engine dies because it no longer has enough electrical power to keep running the fuel injection and ignition systems.

If a jump start gets your car running and it dies again within 5 to 15 minutes of driving, that's a strong sign the alternator is the problem, not the battery.

## The Quick Test

If you have access to a multimeter, you can do a simple test. With the car off, a healthy battery should read between 12.4 and 12.7 volts. Start the car and check again. With the engine running, the voltage should rise to between 13.7 and 14.7 volts if the alternator is working correctly. If it stays at or below 12 volts with the engine running, the alternator is likely not charging.

Most auto parts stores in Las Vegas will also test both your battery and your charging system for free. It takes about five minutes and gives you a clear answer.

## What to Do Based on What You Find

If it's the battery and the car starts with a jump, you can usually drive it directly to a shop or auto parts store to get it tested and replaced. Keep the engine running during the drive since turning it off may leave you stranded again. Avoid running extra electrical loads like AC or the radio if possible.

If it's the alternator, driving is risky. You might make it a few miles before the battery drains completely and the car dies on the road. In that case, calling a tow is the smarter move. Tell the driver where you want to go and let them handle it safely.
