Las Vegas drivers don't think much about cold weather. And most of the year, they don't need to. But December through February brings overnight temperatures that regularly drop into the 30s, and occasionally into the upper 20s in the outer valley and higher elevation areas like Summerlin and the northwest.

That's cold enough to cause real problems for a car that's been sitting outside overnight — especially if the battery is already weak, which in Las Vegas it often is.

Here's what's actually happening when your car won't start on a cold morning, and what you can do about it.

## The Most Common Cause: Your Battery

Cold weather is hard on car batteries. The chemistry inside a lead-acid battery slows down significantly at low temperatures — a battery that's operating at full capacity in mild weather may only deliver 50 to 60 percent of that capacity at freezing temperatures.

This becomes a critical problem in Las Vegas specifically because the extreme summer heat has likely already degraded your battery. Heat is actually more damaging to batteries than cold — it accelerates internal corrosion and causes electrolyte evaporation. A battery that survived Las Vegas summer in a weakened state is especially vulnerable when the temperature drops in January.

The result is a battery that has just enough power to turn the engine over slowly in normal conditions but not enough to fire it up when cold weather cuts its output in half.

**What it sounds like:** A slow, labored cranking sound when you turn the key — or a single click and nothing. If you hear rapid clicking, the battery is almost completely drained.

**What to do:** If you have jumper cables or a jump starter pack, try jumping the vehicle. If it starts and runs normally but struggles the next cold morning, the battery needs to be tested and likely replaced. Most auto parts stores will test your battery for free.

## Engine Oil That's Too Thick

Motor oil is what lubricates all the moving parts inside your engine. In cold temperatures, oil thickens — it flows more slowly and doesn't circulate as efficiently when the engine first starts. A thicker oil puts more strain on the starter motor and makes the engine harder to turn over.

Most modern vehicles and most modern engine oils handle this well, but older vehicles and vehicles that haven't had an oil change in a while are more susceptible. If your oil is overdue for a change and you've been using a conventional oil rather than a full synthetic, cold weather starting can be noticeably harder.

**What it sounds like:** Similar to a weak battery — sluggish cranking, particularly on the first start of the morning.

**What to do:** If your oil is due for a change, get it done. If you're in an older vehicle and consistently struggle with cold starts, talk to your mechanic about switching to a full synthetic oil with a lower cold-weather viscosity rating.

## A Fuel System Issue

Gasoline doesn't freeze at temperatures Las Vegas ever sees, but cold weather can still affect your fuel system in a few ways.

Condensation inside the fuel tank, which is more common when the tank is run low, can introduce small amounts of water into the fuel line. In very cold temperatures, this moisture can freeze in the fuel line and prevent fuel from reaching the engine. Running your tank below a quarter full in winter increases this risk.

Fuel injectors can also be affected by cold — injector deposits that don't cause problems in warm weather may restrict fuel flow enough to cause hard starting when the engine is cold.

**What to do:** Keep your gas tank at least half full during cold months. If you suspect a fuel issue, a fuel injector cleaner added to a full tank can help over time. A persistent hard-start issue that's not battery related is worth having a mechanic check.

## A Failing Starter Motor

The starter motor is what physically cranks the engine when you turn the key. Cold weather puts extra demand on the starter because the engine is harder to turn over — thicker oil, reduced battery output, and generally more resistance in all the mechanical components. A starter that's on its way out may handle normal conditions fine but fail when cold weather adds to the load.

**What it sounds like:** A grinding noise when you turn the key, or a single loud click with no cranking at all. The single click (as opposed to rapid clicking) often indicates the starter solenoid is engaging but the starter motor itself isn't turning.

**What to do:** A failing starter typically needs to be replaced. This is not a roadside fix — if you suspect a starter issue, you need a tow to a mechanic.

## What to Try Before You Call

If your car won't start on a cold Las Vegas morning, run through these steps before calling for help.

Turn off everything drawing power — lights, radio, heat, rear defroster. All of these pull from the battery and reduce what's available for starting. Then wait 60 seconds and try again.

If you have jumper cables and another vehicle available, try a jump start. Let the jumped vehicle run for two to three minutes before attempting to start yours — this gives the battery time to take on some charge. If it starts, drive it for at least 20 minutes to allow the alternator to recharge the battery.

If it won't start after a jump or if you don't have cables, that's when to call.

## Getting Ahead of the Problem

The best time to deal with a battery that's been weakened by Las Vegas summer heat is before the first cold snap of the year — not after you're already stranded in your driveway at 7am.

If your battery is more than three years old, get it tested before winter. It's a free service at most auto parts stores and takes five minutes. Replacing a weak battery proactively costs a fraction of what an emergency call-out and tow costs — and it means you're not dealing with this problem in the dark on a cold January morning.
