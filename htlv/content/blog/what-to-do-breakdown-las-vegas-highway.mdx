Your car just died. You're on the freeway, traffic is flying past your window, and the last thing you need is to be Googling what to do.

That's why we wrote this first.

Whether you broke down on I-15, US-95, the 215, or anywhere else around Las Vegas, this guide walks you through exactly what to do — step by step — so you stay safe and get back on the road as fast as possible.

## Pull Over First, Everything Else Second

The moment you sense something is wrong, start moving right. Even if your car is losing power or making a terrible noise, try to ease it onto the shoulder or the nearest exit ramp before it dies completely.

If you can't get fully off the road, get as far right as possible. Every foot of distance between your car and moving traffic matters.

Once you're stopped, put the car in park and turn on your hazard lights immediately. Don't wait. Do this before you pick up your phone.

A few spots in Las Vegas that are worth knowing about if you break down near them:

**I-15 near the Strip** has extremely heavy traffic and narrow shoulders in some sections. If you break down here, staying in your car is especially important.

**US-95 North toward Summerlin** moves fast, and lighting can be limited at night. Get those hazards on and stay visible.

**The 215 Beltway** has long gaps between exits in some stretches, so you may be waiting a bit further from help than you'd like.

**Boulder Highway** is a mix of locals and tourists and tends to stay congested most of the day.

## Make Yourself Visible to Other Drivers

Once you're stopped, your next job is to make sure other drivers can see you — especially at night or during early morning hours when visibility is lower.

Pop the hood. This is the universal signal that you're broken down, not just parked on the shoulder. It takes five seconds and immediately communicates to passing drivers and law enforcement that something is wrong.

If you have road flares or reflective triangles in your car, set them out about 50 to 100 feet behind your vehicle. This gives approaching drivers time to see you and move over before they're right on top of you.

If you're broken down on a fast-moving highway like I-15, stay inside your vehicle. It sounds counterintuitive but a stationary car is actually safer than standing on the shoulder of a busy freeway. On a slower surface street like Flamingo or Charleston, it's generally okay to get out and stand on the sidewalk or behind a barrier while you wait.

## Call for Help Before You Do Anything Else

Once you're safe, call for help. Don't try to diagnose the problem yourself, don't try to flag down strangers, and don't start walking.

If you're in immediate danger — you're stuck in a traffic lane, there's smoke or fire, or someone is injured — call 911 first.

## Stay Safe While You Wait

You've made the call and help is on the way. Now the job is to wait without putting yourself in danger.

If you're on a highway, stay inside the car with your seatbelt on. Keep your hazard lights running. If someone stops and offers help, crack your window to talk but use your judgment before opening the door. Keep your phone charged — plug it into your car's USB port while you still have power in the vehicle.

If you're on a surface street and it's safe to get out, move away from traffic and stand on a sidewalk or behind a barrier. Keep your belongings with you.

One thing Las Vegas adds to this situation that most breakdown guides don't account for: the heat. In summer, temperatures regularly hit 110°F and above. If you're waiting outside your car, find shade. If you're inside with the engine still running, the A/C is your friend. If the car is completely dead and it's hot, get to shade as quickly as possible and stay hydrated. Heat exhaustion can develop faster than most people expect when you're stressed and standing on asphalt in July.

## Don't Try to Fix It Yourself on a Busy Road

We understand the temptation. But most roadside repairs people attempt on the side of a highway either make things worse or put them in danger.

Changing a tire on a freeway shoulder, for example, is genuinely dangerous. You're crouched next to a moving lane of traffic. It's not worth it.

A few things that are generally reasonable to try on your own:

Calling a friend or family member to bring gas if you've simply run out. Jumping the battery if someone you know has pulled over and you're both comfortable doing it correctly. Checking whether a warning light corresponds to something simple like a loose gas cap.

Leave everything else to the professionals. Tire changes on highways, anything under the vehicle, anything involving the engine, brakes, or transmission — wait for the tow truck. That's what we're here for.
