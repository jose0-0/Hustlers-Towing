If you've called a tow truck in the last ten years, there's a good chance a flatbed showed up. That's not a coincidence. The towing industry has shifted heavily toward flatbed trucks over the past two decades, and the reasons come down to how modern vehicles are built and what happens to them when they're towed the old way.

Here's what the difference actually is, why it matters for your specific vehicle, and when wheel-lift towing is still a reasonable option.

## What the Difference Actually Is

A wheel-lift tow truck uses a metal yoke that hooks under two of the vehicle's wheels — typically the front wheels for a front-wheel-drive car — and lifts that end of the car off the ground. The other two wheels roll on the road as the truck drives. The vehicle is essentially dragged on two wheels for the entire trip.

A flatbed tow truck carries the vehicle on a flat platform that tilts to ground level for loading. The entire vehicle rides on the bed with all four wheels off the road. Nothing rolls. Nothing drags. The car goes from point A to point B as a passenger rather than as a participant.

That distinction matters more than it used to because of how cars are built today.

## Why Modern Cars Need Flatbed Towing

Vehicle design has changed dramatically since the wheel-lift method became standard. Several developments in the last 20 to 30 years have made wheel-lift towing genuinely risky for many vehicles.

**All-wheel drive and four-wheel drive systems.** This is the most critical issue. When a rear-wheel-drive car is towed with its front wheels up and rear wheels rolling, the rear wheels spin freely with no damage because they're not connected to anything under load. But on an all-wheel-drive or four-wheel-drive vehicle, all four wheels are mechanically connected. When the rear wheels roll during a wheel-lift tow, that rotation transmits through the drivetrain to the transmission and transfer case — components that are designed to operate with proper lubrication provided by a running engine. Towing an AWD vehicle on two wheels can cause significant transmission and transfer case damage, sometimes requiring thousands of dollars in repairs.

**Low ground clearance.** Modern performance cars, sports cars, and many sedans sit very low to the ground. The approach angle required to position a wheel-lift yoke under the vehicle can be impossible without scraping or damaging the front air dam, bumper cover, or undercarriage components.

**Electronic parking brakes.** Older vehicles had mechanical parking brakes that could be released manually. Many modern vehicles have electronic parking brakes that require the vehicle to be powered on to release. On a dead vehicle with an engaged electronic parking brake, wheel-lift towing may not be possible without dragging a locked wheel — which causes immediate and obvious tire and brake damage.

**Carbon fiber and composite body components.** High-end vehicles increasingly use materials that don't respond well to the stress points created by wheel-lift towing. The loading forces are concentrated rather than distributed across the vehicle.

## When Wheel-Lift Is Still Fine

Wheel-lift towing isn't inherently dangerous for every vehicle. For older rear-wheel-drive cars, basic front-wheel-drive vehicles without electronic parking brakes, and short-distance moves in controlled environments, wheel-lift is a practical and cost-effective option.

It's also faster to deploy in some situations. For a straightforward move of a basic vehicle in normal conditions, wheel-lift gets the job done without incident.

The problem is that the category of vehicles for which wheel-lift is genuinely safe has shrunk considerably as the overall vehicle fleet has evolved. Most vehicles on Las Vegas roads today are either AWD crossovers, SUVs, or vehicles with enough electronic complexity that flatbed is the right default.

## Questions to Ask Any Towing Company

If you're calling a tow company you haven't used before, these questions are worth asking before you commit.

What type of truck will you send? A company that defaults to wheel-lift for all vehicles without asking about yours is a company that may not be thinking about your drivetrain. Do you have flatbed available for AWD vehicles? Most reputable companies will know immediately why you're asking. Will the vehicle be secured with wheel straps and safety chains? This is standard practice on a flatbed and worth confirming.

A good towing company will answer these questions without hesitation. If they seem annoyed by the question or dismiss the concern, that's useful information about how they operate.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does flatbed towing cost more than wheel-lift?**

Generally yes — flatbed trucks are more expensive to operate and the loading process takes longer. The price difference is usually modest for a standard local tow, and it's worth it for the protection it provides to your vehicle.

**My car is front-wheel drive. Do I still need flatbed?**

Not necessarily — front-wheel-drive vehicles can typically be towed safely with a wheel-lift method with the front wheels up. However, if your vehicle has a low front bumper, an electronic parking brake, or any damage to the front end, flatbed is still the better choice.
