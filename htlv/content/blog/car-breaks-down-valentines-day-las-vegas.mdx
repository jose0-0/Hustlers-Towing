Valentine's weekend in Las Vegas is one of the busiest traffic periods of the year. The Strip is packed, restaurants are fully booked, and more cars are on the road than any typical February weekend. It's also the kind of night where a breakdown goes from inconvenient to genuinely stressful — reservations, plans, and a passenger counting on you.

If your car breaks down this Valentine's weekend, here's exactly what to do.

## Why Breakdowns Spike on Holiday Weekends

It's not a coincidence that more vehicles break down on busy holiday weekends. A few factors combine to make it more likely.

More miles driven. Valentine's weekend means more driving — to restaurants, shows, hotels, and back home late at night. Vehicles that are on the edge of a mechanical issue get pushed over it by the extra use.

Deferred maintenance. There's a well-documented pattern of people putting off car repairs until after the holidays, after a vacation, after a big weekend. Those deferred issues don't disappear — they wait for the worst possible moment.

Cold start failures. February nights in Las Vegas can drop into the 30s. A battery that's been weakened by summer heat and is marginal going into winter may start fine on a warm afternoon but fail after sitting in a cold parking garage for three hours while you're at dinner.

High traffic density. Valentine's weekend Strip traffic means more fender benders, more vehicles pulling over, and more congestion that makes everything take longer — including tow truck response.

## The First Thing to Do

Pull over safely. This applies whether your car is making a noise, a warning light has come on, or the engine has completely died. Get to the right shoulder or the nearest parking lot. Valentine's weekend traffic on the Strip moves slowly enough that a disabled vehicle in a traffic lane creates an immediate and dangerous backup.

If you're on a surface street in a restaurant district — think Summerlin, downtown, Henderson, the Arts District — a parking lot or side street is almost always within reach. Use it.

Once you're stopped, hazard lights on, and then assess.

## Assess Before You Call

A few quick checks before calling a tow can save you time and money if the problem is simple.

Is the battery the issue? If the car won't start or died while driving and all the warning lights came on at once, a dead battery is the most likely cause. If you have jumper cables and there's another vehicle nearby — a parking attendant, a good Samaritan — a jump start may get you going.

Did you run out of gas? It happens, especially on busy nights when you're focused on everything else. Check your gauge. If you're empty, fuel delivery is faster and cheaper than a tow.

Is it a warning light without other symptoms? If a light came on but the car is running and driving normally, it may be safe to continue to your destination and deal with it the next day. Check the owner's manual or a quick search to determine whether the specific light indicates an immediate risk.

If none of these apply — if the car won't run, is making serious mechanical noises, or you're not confident in what you're seeing — call us.

## How to Salvage the Night

A breakdown doesn't have to end the evening entirely. A few practical moves can turn a stressful situation into just a story you'll tell later.

Call the restaurant or venue. Most Las Vegas restaurants and venues on Valentine's weekend are accustomed to traffic delays and last-minute situations. A quick call explaining that you've had a car breakdown will often get you rescheduled to a later time or held for a reasonable window. A "we're stuck, we'll be there in 45 minutes" call is much better than a no-show.

Use a rideshare. If the tow truck is on the way and you're somewhere safe, there's no reason to sit on the curb together. Leave the car — making sure you've communicated clearly with the tow driver about where the vehicle will be going — and catch a rideshare to continue your evening. The vehicle will be handled.

Stay warm and safe. February nights in Las Vegas get cold quickly after dark. If you're waiting outside, have a jacket. If you're waiting in the car, the heater is your friend if the car will still run accessories.

## After the Tow

Make sure you know where your vehicle is being taken before the truck leaves. Get the driver's name and a receipt. Confirm the destination address. If your mechanic isn't open until Monday, confirm that the tow yard or shop can secure the vehicle over the weekend.

Most mechanics in Las Vegas have after-hours drop arrangements. A tow driver can drop the vehicle in the lot with the keys in the drop box if you've arranged it in advance.
