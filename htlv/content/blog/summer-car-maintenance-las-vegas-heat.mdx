Las Vegas summer is not like summer anywhere else. When the temperature climbs past 110 degrees and the asphalt is hot enough to fry an egg, your car is dealing with conditions most vehicles were not specifically engineered for. The cooling system is working harder, the battery is under stress, the tires are expanding, and the rubber and plastic components throughout the engine bay are aging faster than they would in a temperate climate.

Most people know to check their oil and make sure their AC works. But there are eight things that consistently get missed, and they are the ones we see causing actual breakdowns when summer hits in full force.

## 1. The Coolant Concentration

Checking that you have coolant is not the same as checking that your coolant is the right mixture. Coolant is a mix of antifreeze and water, and the ratio matters. The standard is roughly 50/50, but in extreme heat some mechanics recommend a slightly higher antifreeze concentration for Las Vegas driving.

More importantly, coolant degrades over time. Old coolant becomes acidic and actually starts to corrode the components it is supposed to protect. If you cannot remember the last time you had a coolant flush, summer is the time to do it.

## 2. The Radiator Cap

This one gets overlooked constantly. The radiator cap is a pressure valve that keeps the cooling system sealed. A cap that is not sealing properly allows coolant to escape as steam and lets air into the system, which causes hot spots in the engine. Radiator caps are inexpensive and easy to replace, and a weak cap is a common cause of overheating in older vehicles.

## 3. Battery Condition

Most people associate battery problems with cold weather. In reality, heat kills batteries faster than cold does. High temperatures cause internal corrosion and evaporation of the electrolyte solution inside the battery. A battery that is three to four years old going into a Las Vegas summer is a battery that may not make it through to fall.

Get it tested before the heat arrives. Free at any auto parts store, five minutes of your time, and it tells you definitively whether your battery is healthy or on the edge.

## 4. Tire Pressure Including the Spare

Heat causes the air inside your tires to expand. A tire that was correctly inflated in the morning can be noticeably over-inflated by afternoon if the car has been sitting in direct sun. Overinflated tires have less contact patch with the road and are more prone to blowouts from road hazards.

Check pressure in the morning before driving. Do not let air out of a warm tire to reach the correct number — wait until the tire is cool, then check it. And check the spare while you are at it. A flat spare discovered at the moment you need it is a genuinely bad situation.

## 5. Wiper Blades

Las Vegas summer brings monsoon storms, usually starting in July. A monsoon downpour on the 215 with wiper blades that have been baking in the desert sun all spring is a visibility disaster. Summer UV exposure and heat cracks and hardens rubber wiper blades quickly. Replace them before monsoon season rather than after the first storm reveals they are useless.

## 6. The AC Cabin Air Filter

Your cabin air filter is what the air passes through before it reaches you inside the car. In Las Vegas, dust, pollen, and desert particulates clog these filters faster than in most cities. A heavily clogged cabin air filter reduces AC airflow noticeably, which means the AC has to work harder to cool the cabin, which puts more load on the engine.

Cabin air filters are typically located behind the glove box and can be replaced in about 10 minutes with no tools. Check yours before summer. If it looks dark and matted with debris, replace it.

## 7. Drive Belts and Hoses

The serpentine belt drives the alternator, water pump, and AC compressor. Hoses carry coolant throughout the engine. Both are made of rubber, and both deteriorate significantly faster in extreme heat and UV exposure.

Do a visual inspection of any belt or hose you can see under the hood. Look for cracking, fraying on belt edges, or any hose that feels hard and brittle or soft and spongy rather than firm but pliable. Either condition means replacement is coming soon. Better to do it before summer than to have a belt snap on the freeway in August.

## 8. Engine Air Filter

Different from the cabin air filter. The engine air filter is what keeps dust and debris out of the engine itself. Las Vegas is dusty and engine air filters here clog faster than in most climates. A clogged engine air filter restricts airflow to the engine, reduces fuel efficiency, and can cause rough idling or hesitation under load.

Pull it out and hold it up to the light. If you cannot see light through it, it needs to be replaced. It is a 5-minute job on most vehicles and the filter itself is typically under $25.

## The Bigger Picture

None of these checks are complicated, and most of them can be done in a Saturday morning. The ones that need professional attention — a coolant flush, a belt replacement, AC service — are worth scheduling before the heat arrives in full force rather than after a breakdown on the freeway when temperatures are past 110.
