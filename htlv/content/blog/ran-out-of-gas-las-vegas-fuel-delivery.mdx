Running out of gas feels like it should not happen to adults, which is probably why people are more embarrassed about it than almost any other roadside situation. But it happens to people every single day in Las Vegas, and the reasons are usually pretty reasonable — distracted by a long drive, unfamiliar with how fast a rental car burns through fuel, a faulty fuel gauge that reads higher than the actual level, or just misjudging how far the next gas station is on a desert highway.

Whatever the reason, the solution is straightforward: fuel delivery. Here is exactly how it works and what to expect.

## What Fuel Delivery Actually Is

Fuel delivery is a roadside service where a technician drives to your location with enough gasoline to get your car to the nearest gas station. It is not a full tank — the amount delivered is typically two to three gallons, which is enough to drive several miles to a station where you can fill up properly.

The technician brings the right type of fuel for your vehicle — regular unleaded for most cars, premium for vehicles that require it. If you drive a diesel vehicle, make sure to mention that when you call because diesel and gasoline are stored and transported separately.

## How Long It Takes

In Las Vegas proper, fuel delivery response times are similar to other roadside services — roughly 30 to 45 minutes under normal conditions, longer during peak hours or high-demand periods like weekend nights and holiday weekends.

If you are on a highway outside the city on the way to or from Las Vegas, response time will be longer depending on where you are. On US-93 heading toward Kingman or on I-15 heading toward California, there may be limited service options nearby. Knowing where you are relative to the nearest town helps when you call.

## What It Costs

Fuel delivery service typically includes a service call fee plus the cost of the fuel itself. The service fee covers the technician's time and travel. In Las Vegas, a fuel delivery call generally runs in the $50 to $80 range for the service fee, plus the cost of the two to three gallons delivered.

This is significantly cheaper than a tow, which is the alternative if fuel delivery is not available. Getting your car towed to the nearest gas station because it is out of fuel is a situation that is frustrating and expensive and easily avoided.

## What to Do While You Wait

Get safely off the road first. If you felt the car starting to sputter or lose power on the highway, coast to the right shoulder or the nearest exit. Do not push it — an out-of-gas car that dies while you are still in a lane is much worse than one that dies on the shoulder.

Turn on your hazard lights as soon as you are stopped. If you have reflective triangles, place them behind the vehicle.

If it is hot and you are waiting in direct sun, find shade if there is any nearby. Stay hydrated. Las Vegas summer heat can cause heat exhaustion faster than most people expect when you are stressed and outside.

Keep your phone charged. If the car is still able to run accessories on remaining battery power, charge your phone. You may need it to communicate with the dispatcher or to let someone know where you are.

## A Few Things Worth Knowing

Running completely dry is harder on modern fuel-injected engines than it used to be. The fuel pump in most modern vehicles is cooled and lubricated by the fuel itself. Running the tank completely empty can cause the fuel pump to run hot briefly, which over time contributes to wear. It will not destroy your fuel pump on one occasion, but it is worth not making a habit of it.

After receiving fuel delivery, drive directly to a gas station without turning the engine off if possible. You want to make sure the fuel has circulated properly through the system before the next start cycle.

If your car does not start after fuel delivery, the issue may not just be the fuel. Low fuel combined with a weak battery, for example, can create a situation where the car will not start even after fuel is added. If that happens, the technician can assess what else might be needed.

## Can I Walk to Get Gas Instead?

Technically yes, but we do not recommend it in Las Vegas for a few reasons. Walking on a highway shoulder is dangerous. In summer, walking any significant distance in extreme heat creates a real risk of heat-related illness. And gasoline containers are limited in how much they can legally carry — a small container from a gas station may not hold enough to get your car started and to a station.

The simpler and safer option is to stay with the car and call for fuel delivery.
