Most of the towing conversation focuses on passenger vehicles because that is what most drivers own. But Las Vegas sits at the intersection of two major interstate highways and is a significant logistics hub for the Southwest. I-15 and US-95 carry enormous volumes of commercial truck traffic, and the metro area itself has a large population of RV owners, fleet vehicle operators, and businesses that run box trucks, work vans, and other commercial vehicles.

When one of those vehicles breaks down, a standard tow truck is not the answer. Here is what heavy-duty towing involves, what vehicles require it, and what you need to know before you call.

## What Qualifies as Heavy-Duty Towing

Heavy-duty towing generally refers to the recovery and transport of vehicles that exceed the capacity of a standard light-duty tow truck. In practical terms, this includes semi-trucks and 18-wheelers, large RVs and motorhomes, box trucks and delivery vehicles, buses and shuttle vehicles, large construction equipment that is road-legal, and oversized commercial trailers.

The distinction matters because the equipment required is completely different. A light-duty flatbed or wheel-lift truck that handles passenger cars weighs roughly 26,000 pounds and has a towing capacity of around 10,000 to 15,000 pounds. A heavy-duty rotator or underlift tow truck can weigh over 60,000 pounds and handle loads exceeding 100,000 pounds. Using the wrong equipment on a heavy vehicle is not just ineffective — it is dangerous.

## Semi-Trucks and 18-Wheelers

Commercial trucks break down on Nevada interstates regularly. I-15 between Las Vegas and the California border and US-93 heading north carry constant commercial traffic, and the combination of long hauls, extreme heat, and the grades on those roads creates genuine stress on vehicles that are already working hard.

When a semi breaks down, the situation involves more than just the truck. There may be a loaded trailer that needs to be handled separately. There may be a hazmat situation if the trailer is carrying regulated materials. There may be a jackknife or rollover scenario that requires a rotator — a specialized heavy wrecker with a rotating crane arm — to upright and recover the vehicle.

Semi-truck towing also involves coordination with the trucking company's dispatch, the driver's fleet management, and sometimes the shipper if there is a load integrity concern. An experienced heavy-duty towing operator knows how to navigate this and who needs to be contacted.

## RVs and Motorhomes

Las Vegas is a popular destination and a common stopping point for cross-country RV travelers on I-15 coming from California and heading toward Utah and the national parks. Class A motorhomes, fifth-wheel trailers, and large travel trailers all present towing challenges that standard equipment cannot handle.

A Class A motorhome can weigh between 20,000 and 30,000 pounds fully loaded. The wheelbase is long, the turning radius is wide, and the chassis configuration requires specific hookup points. A standard tow truck driver who primarily handles passenger vehicles will not necessarily know how to approach this correctly.

For fifth-wheel and travel trailers, the situation depends on whether the tow vehicle is also disabled. If the truck or SUV pulling the trailer is functional and only the trailer has a problem — a blown tire, an axle issue — that is a different situation than a scenario where the entire rig needs to be moved.

## Box Trucks and Commercial Vans

Many Las Vegas businesses operate box trucks, delivery vans, and work vehicles that fall in the medium-duty range — heavier than a standard pickup truck but lighter than a semi. These vehicles typically require a medium-duty tow truck rather than a standard light-duty or full heavy-duty wrecker.

For businesses running fleets, a breakdown can mean delivery delays, schedule disruptions, and direct revenue impact. Quick response and getting the vehicle to the right mechanic efficiently is a priority, not just getting it moved.

## What to Have Ready When You Call for Heavy-Duty Towing

The information you share upfront directly affects how quickly the right equipment gets dispatched.

Tell us what type of vehicle you have and its approximate weight or class. Semi or box truck. Class A motorhome or fifth-wheel. Single axle or tandem axle. Loaded or empty if it is a commercial vehicle.

Tell us the exact location including the highway, direction, and mile marker if you are on a freeway. Heavy-duty tow trucks take longer to position and set up than light-duty trucks, and the driver needs to approach from the right direction.

Tell us if the vehicle is upright and accessible or if there is a rollover, jackknife, or off-road recovery situation. These require different equipment and a more complex response.
