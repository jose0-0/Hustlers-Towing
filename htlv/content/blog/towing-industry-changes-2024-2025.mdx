If the last time you thought carefully about towing was five years ago, you are working from an outdated picture. The industry has gone through a significant amount of change since 2020, driven by technology shifts, cost pressures, regulatory attention, and the evolution of the vehicles that need to be towed.

Most of these changes affect what you experience as a driver when something goes wrong on the road. Here is a practical breakdown of what has shifted and what it means for you.

## Prices Have Gone Up and the Reasons Are Real

The most noticeable change for most drivers is cost. Towing rates across the country have increased meaningfully since 2020, and Las Vegas is no exception. We covered the specific current pricing in our towing prices article, but the why is worth understanding here.

The pandemic-era supply chain disruption hit the towing industry the same way it hit everything else. Truck parts, tires, and equipment became harder to source and more expensive. New tow truck prices increased significantly — a well-equipped flatbed that cost around $80,000 in 2019 now runs well over $120,000 in many configurations.

Insurance costs for towing operators have climbed substantially. The liability exposure in roadside work is significant, and insurers adjusted their pricing accordingly. Fleet insurance for a towing company is a major operating expense that went up sharply in the early 2020s.

Fuel costs rose and then partially pulled back, but diesel — the fuel that runs tow trucks — has remained elevated relative to pre-2020 levels. That shows up directly in operating costs.

Labor market shifts hit towing companies hard. Finding, training, and retaining qualified drivers became more competitive and more expensive across the service sector. Towing requires specific skills and the willingness to work irregular hours in challenging conditions. Companies that want good drivers have to pay for them.

## Technology Has Changed How Dispatch Works

The dispatch side of towing has been heavily affected by technology over the past five years, in ways that are both positive and worth being cautious about.

On the positive side, GPS tracking and real-time dispatch systems have made it possible for better-run companies to give more accurate ETAs and route drivers more efficiently. The best operators in 2025 can tell you with reasonable precision where their truck is and when it will arrive, because they actually know.

On the more complicated side, the rise of on-demand dispatch platforms — essentially the Uber model applied to towing — has created a marketplace of independent contractors that some national call centers use to handle roadside calls. When you call a number and someone answers quickly with a low quote and a short estimated arrival, there is a decent chance you are talking to a broker who is about to call a contractor to find someone available. This model creates the response time and pricing uncertainty that frustrates drivers.

Recognizing whether you are talking to a direct operator or a broker is one of the most useful skills a driver can have. A direct operator can tell you specifically which truck is being sent and where it is. A broker can only tell you what they hope will happen.

## Vehicles Have Gotten More Complex

The vehicles being towed have changed substantially, and the industry has had to adapt.

The shift to all-wheel drive as the default drivetrain in crossovers and SUVs — which now dominate new vehicle sales — changed what tow method is appropriate for most vehicles. Wheel-lift towing on AWD vehicles risks transmission and transfer case damage, as we covered in our flatbed towing article. Companies that have not updated their practices to reflect this are creating liability for themselves and damage risk for their customers.

Electric vehicles add another layer of complexity. EVs have high-voltage battery packs that require specific handling during accident recovery and towing. The battery can continue to pose risks even after a crash. EV-specific towing protocols have developed over the past few years as the number of EVs on Nevada roads has grown, and not every operator has kept up.

The increasing prevalence of low-profile tires on modern vehicles has also changed how wheel straps are applied. Thin sidewalls are more vulnerable to strap damage, and operators who use standard strap placement on very low-profile tires are risking sidewall damage.

## Regulatory Attention Has Increased

Following high-profile cases of predatory towing practices in cities across the country, state and local regulators have increased scrutiny of the industry. In Nevada, complaints to the Nevada Transportation Authority about towing companies have increased, and the agency has become more active in investigating billing complaints and operating violations.

This is a positive development for consumers. It means there is a clearer pathway for drivers who believe they were overcharged or mistreated, and that pathway has real consequences for companies that abuse it.

The attention has also pushed more legitimate operators to be more transparent and documented about their pricing and practices, which benefits everyone.

## What This Means for You as a Driver

The practical takeaways are straightforward.

Expect to pay more than you would have in 2018 or 2019 for the same tow. Prices are up across the industry for real reasons, and a rate that seems very low relative to the current market is a flag worth paying attention to.

Know the difference between a direct operator and a dispatch broker before you commit to a call. Ask directly whether the company owns its trucks and employs its drivers.

Verify that any company you use is aware of the specific requirements for your vehicle, particularly if you drive an AWD, EV, or exotic vehicle.

And know that you have real recourse if something goes wrong. Nevada's regulatory framework gives you options beyond just leaving a bad review.
